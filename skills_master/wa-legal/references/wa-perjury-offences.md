## Chapter XVI — Offences Relating to the Administration of Justice

### s.120 — Definition: "Judicial Proceeding"

"Judicial proceeding" includes **any proceeding in which evidence may be taken on oath**.

- Broad definition — covers courts, tribunals, royal commissions, and other bodies where sworn evidence is taken
- Does not require a properly constituted court in the traditional sense

---

### s.124 — Perjury (Indictable)

**Offence:** Knowingly giving false testimony on a material matter in any judicial proceeding.

**Elements:**
1. In a judicial proceeding (broadly defined under s.120)
2. Knowingly gives false testimony
3. On a matter material to any question depending in that proceeding

**Immaterial considerations (NOT required to prove perjury):**
- Whether the testimony was given on oath or other sanction
- Whether oral or written testimony
- Whether the witness was competent
- Whether the testimony was admissible
- Whether the court was properly constituted

**Key point:** Perjury can be committed even in irregular or defectively constituted proceedings, provided it is a "judicial proceeding" under s.120.

---

### s.125 — Penalty for Perjury

| Circumstance | Penalty |
|--------------|----------|
| Basic perjury (s.124) | **14 years imprisonment** |
| Aggravated perjury (s.125(2)) | **Life imprisonment** |

**Aggravated perjury (s.125(2)):**
If the perjury was committed **to procure the conviction of another person for a crime punishable with life imprisonment**, the offender is liable to **life imprisonment**.

---

### s.127 — False Evidence Before Royal Commission

**Offence:** Knowingly giving a false answer to a lawful question relevant to the subject of inquiry before:
- A Royal Commission
- A commissioner or other person authorised to take evidence

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty (typically significant imprisonment).

---

### s.129 — Fabricating Evidence

**Offence:** With intent to mislead any tribunal:
- Fabricating evidence; or
- Knowing that evidence is fabricated, using it in a judicial proceeding

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.130 — Corruption of Witness

**Offence:** Inducing or endeavouring to induce any person:
- To give false testimony; or
- To withhold true testimony

**Applies to:** Any judicial proceeding or tribunal where evidence on oath may be taken.

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.131 — Deceiving Witness

**Offence:** Using any fraud or deceit to affect:
- The testimony of any witness; or
- The conduct of any person in relation to any judicial proceeding

**Examples:**
- Forging documents to mislead a witness
- Concealing evidence to affect witness testimony
- Using deception to influence how a witness testifies

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.132 — Destroying Evidence

**Offence:** Wilfully destroying any evidence that the person **knows or believes** is required in a judicial proceeding (whether or not the proceeding has commenced).

**Key points:**
- Covers destruction, concealment, or alteration of evidence
- Applies even if the proceeding has not yet commenced, provided the person knows/believes it will be required
- "Required in a judicial proceeding" includes documents, objects, digital evidence

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.133 — Preventing Witness From Attending

**Offence:** Wilfully preventing or endeavouring to prevent a person who has been summoned as a witness from attending as a witness in any judicial proceeding.

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.133A — False Prosecution

**Offence:** Knowingly signing a prosecution notice that the person knows is false.

**Context:** Applies to prosecution notices under the *Criminal Procedure Act 2004 (WA)*.

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.134 — Conspiracy for False Prosecution

**Offence:** Conspiring to charge an innocent person with an offence.

**Elements:**
- Agreement between two or more persons
- Intention to charge an innocent person
- The person charged must be innocent of the offence

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.135 — Conspiring to Pervert Course of Justice

**Offence:** Conspiring to pervert the course of justice (not amounting to an offence under s.134).

**Examples:**
- Conspiring to hide evidence
- Conspiring to intimidate witnesses
- Conspiring to provide false alibis

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.143 — Attempting to Pervert Course of Justice

**Offence:** Attempting, by any means other than those specified in ss.129–135, to pervert the course of justice.

**Broad provision:** Catches attempts to pervert justice that don't fit neatly into the specific offences above.

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

## Chapter XX — Miscellaneous Offences Against Public Authority

### s.169(1) — False Statement on Oath

**Offence:** Knowingly making a false statement on oath on a **material particular**.

**Key differences from perjury (s.124):**
- Does NOT require a "judicial proceeding"
- Requires "on oath" (perjury does not)
- Covers statutory declarations, affidavits for court, and other sworn statements

**Examples:**
- False statement in an affidavit filed with a court
- False statement in a statutory declaration
- False statement on any other sworn document

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.169(2) — False Statutory Declaration

**Offence:** Knowingly making a false statement in a **statutory declaration**.

**Distinction from s.169(1):**
- Specifically applies to statutory declarations (not other sworn statements)
- Does not require the statement to be "on oath" in the traditional sense

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

---

### s.170 — False Information to Official

**Offence:** Knowingly giving **false information** required by or under any written law to:
- A public authority; or
- A person performing functions under written law

**Examples:**
- Providing false information to police in a statement
- Providing false information to a government department in a statutory form
- Providing false information required by regulation

**Penalty:** Check current Criminal Code for penalty.

## Key Elements Summary — Perjury (s.124)

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|--------------|
| **Proceeding** | Must be a "judicial proceeding" (broadly defined, s.120) |
| **State of mind** | "Knowingly" — actual knowledge of falsity (not recklessness or negligence) |
| **Conduct** | Giving false testimony (oral or written) |
| **Materiality** | On a matter material to a question depending in the proceeding |
| **Immaterial** | Oath/non-oath, oral/written, competence, admissibility, court constitution |

**Proving "knowingly":**
- Direct evidence (e.g., admission)
- Circumstantial evidence (e.g., prior consistent statements, documents showing knowledge of truth)
- Inconsistent statements
- Corroborating evidence

---

## Aggravated Perjury (s.125(2)) — Life Imprisonment

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|--------------|
| **Basic perjury** | s.124 perjury established |
| **Purpose** | To procure the conviction of another person |
| **Target offence** | Crime punishable with life imprisonment |

**Examples of predicate offences punishable by life:**
- Murder
- Wilful murder
- Trafficking large commercial quantities of drugs (certain offences)
- Certain sexual offences against children

## Defences and Mitigating Factors

### Strict Liability vs Mens Rea

s.124 perjury requires **"knowingly"** — this is a high mens rea threshold:
- Must know the testimony is false
- Recklessness (not caring whether true or false) is insufficient
- Negligence (should have known) is insufficient
- Genuine mistake, even unreasonable mistake, is a defence if there was no "knowing"

### Corroboration Warnings

While not strictly required as a matter of law in WA, perjury is often treated with caution where there is:
- Single witness accusation
- No corroborating evidence
- Unreliable complainant

### Mitigating Factors (Sentencing)

- No prior convictions
- Early guilty plea
- Remorse and insight
- Cooperation with authorities
- Minimal impact / no actual miscarriage of justice resulted
- Underlying stress, pressure, or coercion (limited mitigation for perjury)

### Aggravating Factors (Sentencing)

- Perjury to procure conviction for serious offence (s.125(2) — life imprisonment)
- Perjury that actually caused miscarriage of justice
- Perjury in serious criminal trial (leading to wrongful conviction)
- Pattern of dishonesty / prior convictions for dishonesty offences
- Perjury for financial gain
- Perjury to cover up other serious offences
- Abuse of professional or public office

## Procedure and Jurisdiction

### Who Prosecutes?

- **WA Police** investigate perjury and false statement offences
- **Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP WA)** typically prosecutes indictable perjury matters
- **Magistrates Court** can hear perjury summarily if both parties agree (less common given seriousness)
- **District Court** is the typical venue for indictable perjury trials
- **Supreme Court** for the most serious cases (e.g., perjury leading to wrongful life sentence)

### Indictable vs Summary

| Offence | Typical venue | Notes |
|---------|--------------|-------|
| s.124 perjury | District Court (indictable) | Can be heard summarily in Magistrates Court with consent |
| s.125 penalty | District/Supreme Court | Aggravated perjury (life) must be Supreme Court |
| s.127 false evidence (Royal Commission) | District Court | Serious matter; indictable |
| s.129 fabricating evidence | District Court | Indictable |
| s.130 corrupting witness | District Court | Indictable |
| s.169 false statement/oath | Magistrates Court (summary) | Less serious; typically summary |
| s.169(2) false statutory declaration | Magistrates Court (summary) | Often dealt with summarily |

### Limitation Periods

- **Indictable offences (perjury, fabricating evidence, etc.):** Generally **no limitation period** — can be prosecuted at any time
- **Summary offences:** Typically **12 months** from the date of the offence (Criminal Procedure Act 2004 s.39)
- s.169 false statement/oath is typically prosecuted summarily — 12-month limitation applies

## Practice Notes — WA Courts

### Magistrates Court (Summary Perjury / False Statements)

- Typically only less serious false statement matters (s.169)
- Early plea often attracts significant sentence discount
- Character references relevant to sentence
- Prior convictions for dishonesty are highly relevant

### District Court (Indictable Perjury)

- Most common venue for s.124 perjury trials
- Jury trial available (unless waived)
- Disclosure of prosecution brief required
- Can result in significant imprisonment (up to 14 years)

### Supreme Court (Aggravated Perjury — Life Imprisonment)

- s.125(2) — perjury to procure life-imprisonment conviction
- Most serious perjury offence
- Typically judge-alone trial (exceptional case for jury)
- Maximum penalty: life imprisonment

## Checklist: Perjury Defence (Accused)

- [ ] Obtain full prosecution brief / disclosure
- [ ] Identify the specific testimony alleged to be false
- [ ] Identify the "knowing" element — what evidence shows the accused knew it was false?
- [ ] Identify corroboration (or lack thereof) for the prosecution case
- [ ] Gather all evidence showing the statement was true (or honestly believed)
- [ ] Identify prior inconsistent statements by key prosecution witnesses
- [ ] Consider alibi, recollection, or mistake defences
- [ ] Prepare character references (if pleading guilty / sentencing)
- [ ] Consider whether the matter should be heard summarily (Magistrates) or on indictment (District/Supreme)
- [ ] Apply for legal aid (1300 650 579) immediately if eligible

---

## Checklist: Perjury Prosecution (Victim / Complainant)

- [ ] Report to WA Police immediately — obtain incident number
- [ ] Preserve all evidence (original documents, recordings, emails)
- [ ] Identify witnesses who heard the false testimony
- [ ] Identify evidence that proves the statement was false
- [ ] Identify evidence that proves the accused knew it was false
- [ ] Prepare chronology of events and statements
- [ ] Obtain legal advice about civil remedies (defamation, malicious prosecution) if relevant
- [ ] Consider DPP referral if police do not proceed
