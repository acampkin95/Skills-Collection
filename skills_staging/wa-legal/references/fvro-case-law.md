# WA FVRO Case Law Research Guide

> **DISCLAIMER**: General legal information only. Not legal advice. Retrieve and read the full judgment before relying on any case in submissions.

## Verification Rule

Do not cite FVRO/VRO case law from memory, commentary, or benchbook summaries. Before using any case:

1. Retrieve the full judgment from AustLII, Jade, Westlaw/Lexis, or an official court site.
2. Confirm party names, neutral citation, court, date, and paragraph references.
3. Check the current Restraining Orders Act 1997 (WA), especially ss.45, 46, 61, 61A, 61B, 61C, and 62.
4. Identify whether the proposition is ratio, sentencing guidance, statutory interpretation, or fact-specific comment.
5. Check whether later legislation or appellate authority changes the proposition.

Use this table as a research checklist, not as authority by itself.

| Candidate case | Topic to verify |

| *Roe v D'Costa* [2014] WASCA 118 | s.61A mandatory imprisonment preconditions |
| *Wallam v Grosveld* [2015] WASC 145 | Protected-person invitations, victim-blaming language, breach sentencing |
| *Conomy v Western Australian Police* [2016] WASCA 31 | Text messages and apparently innocuous contact as possible breach conduct |
| *Gull v Barker* [2013] WASC 398 | Interim order threshold and procedural fairness issues |

## Safer Statutory Starting Points

When a user asks about breach, protected-person contact, or variation:

- Start with the exact order terms and the current Act.
- Treat protected-person contact as a fact relevant to statutory analysis, not as a general defence.
- Separate liability, mitigation, sentencing, and variation/cancellation.
- If the user is charged, arrested, on bail, or facing imprisonment, stop drafting strategy and refer for urgent legal advice.
