# Interpersonal Matter Hardening

Rules to protect affidavit credibility by separating legally relevant facts from narrative background. Use this reference for family law, domestic violence, FVRO, and coercive control matters.

**Load this reference during:**
- Phase 6 (build_chronology)
- Phase 8 (draft_affidavit)
- Phase 13 (adversarial_review)

---

## 1. Relationship Background vs Incident Facts

**Rule:** Separate relationship context from specific incidents. Keep background neutral; focus evidence on dated events.

**Background (context only):**
- How the relationship developed
- General patterns and duration
- Emotional impact on affiant
- Keep separate section; minimal detail; neutral tone

**Incident facts (core evidence):**
- Specific dated events
- Direct communications or behaviours
- Corroborating sources (recordings, messages, police reports, medical records)
- Chronological structure
- Evidence-based language

### Example: Weak Approach
*"We had a long relationship where he was controlling. He tried to isolate me from friends and family over the whole time we were together. This made me feel very trapped. One time he didn't let me see my sister."*

**Problems:**
- "Controlling" is interpretation, not fact
- "Long time" undefined
- "Tried to isolate" lacks specificity or evidence
- Single example overshadows vague pattern claim
- Emotional language ("trapped") unsupported

### Example: Strong Approach
*"The parties were in a de facto relationship from January 2022 to August 2025 (3.5 years).*

*In February 2023, respondent told the affiant, 'You can't go to your sister's birthday.' SMS exchange dated 15 February 2023 [EV-012] confirms affiant's request and respondent's refusal. In March 2023, respondent repeated this pattern: refusing affiant's request to attend her mother's medical appointment. In May 2023, respondent stated in person, 'I don't want you seeing your family anymore.' These three documented instances over a 4-month period demonstrate a pattern of respondent restricting affiant's access to family.*

*Impact on affiant: affiant attended no family events between March 2023 and July 2024. Police welfare check, July 2024 [EV-015], noted affiant's report of social isolation.*"

**Strengths:**
- Background concise and dated (3.5 years)
- Three specific incidents with dates
- Direct quotes where available
- Evidence references (EV-IDs)
- Pattern threshold met (3+ instances)
- Corroboration cited (SMS, police record)
- Impact stated factually with evidence

## 2. Distinguish Direct Fact from Interpretation

**Rule:** Use direct fact. Note interpretation as inference with source and confidence level.

**Direct fact:** Observable behaviour, communication, or documented state.
```
Example: "On 12 March, respondent said, 'I will not pay you.'"
```

**Interpretation:** Conclusion about motive, intent, or meaning.
```
Example: "Respondent was deliberately trying to control me."
```

**Correct approach:**
- Lead with direct fact
- If interpretation is relevant, add as inference: "This suggests respondent intended to restrict financial independence (Medium confidence: direct statement, no other evidence of intent)."

### Reference Table: Direct Fact vs Interpretation

| Emotional / Interpretation | Direct Fact | Correct Phrasing |
|---|---|---|
| "He was deliberately cruel to me" | Respondent hit affiant on 5 March | "On 5 March, respondent hit affiant. Affiant sustained bruising to left arm (photographed 6 March, EV-008). This suggests an intent to cause harm or fear (Medium confidence)." |
| "She was trying to destroy me financially" | Respondent withdrew $15,000 from joint account without notice; affiant's rent cheque bounced | "On 12 April, respondent withdrew $15,000 from joint account (Bank statement, EV-019). Affiant's rent cheque bounced the same day. This pattern suggests respondent acted to impede affiant's financial security (Medium confidence: action is documented; intent inferred from timing and affiant's account)." |
| "He made me feel terrified" | Respondent stood over affiant and raised fist; did not strike | "On 20 June, respondent stood approximately 30cm away from affiant, raised his fist, and held it for 10 seconds before lowering it. No contact was made. Affiant experienced fear and left the residence. (High confidence: direct observation; Medium confidence on affiant's emotional state absent contemporary evidence like text/call to friend or medical note)." |
| "She was abusive" | Respondent repeatedly called affiant "stupid" in text messages; called affiant's child "useless" in front of others | "Between March and July 2025, respondent sent 47 text messages containing derogatory language (SMS thread, EV-031). Examples include 'You're stupid' (18 March), 'That's pathetic' (2 May). Affiant's cousin was present on 10 July when respondent told affiant's 8-year-old child, 'You're useless.' (Cousin confirmed in email, 11 July, EV-032)." |
| "He was controlling" | Respondent checked affiant's phone daily; demanded affiant's location at all times; set curfew | "Respondent required affiant to provide mobile phone password and conducted daily reviews of messages and call logs (Affiant testimony; no contemporaneous record). Respondent required affiant to disclose location continuously via Google Maps (Screenshots of shared location, EV-045, dated November 2024–March 2025). Respondent stated curfew of 18:00 in household rules document dated 1 December 2024 (EV-050)." |

## 3. Handle Allegations with Care Markers

**Rule:** Confidence marking protects credibility. Use High/Medium/Low confidence labels internally during drafting; remove before filing.

### Confidence Levels

**High confidence:** Supported by police report, medical record, or contemporaneous communication.
```
Example (High):
"On 8 March, respondent struck affiant on the face. Affiant attended Dr Singh at Medic Centre
the same day (Medical report 8 March 2026, EV-007; injury consistent with blunt force trauma)."
```

**Medium confidence:** Affiant testimony with at least one corroborating source.
```
Example (Medium):
"Respondent told affiant on 15 March, 'I'm taking the children.' Affiant immediately texted
her mother: 'R just threatened to take the kids' (SMS 15 March 3:47pm, EV-019).
Affiant has no police report or recording of the original statement."
```

**Low confidence:** Memory-only; no contemporaneous record.
```
Example (Low):
"Respondent told affiant in approximately July 2023 that he did not want affiant to work.
Affiant has no contemporaneous record (no text, email, or witness note from that time)."
```

### Usage and Removal

**During drafting (internal use only):**
```
On 8 March, respondent struck affiant on the face. (HIGH: Medical report 8 March, EV-007)
Affiant recalls respondent saying on or about 15 March, "I'm going to take the children." (MEDIUM: SMS
to mother same day, EV-019; no direct recording)
In approximately July 2023, respondent told affiant he did not want her to work. (LOW: Memory-only)
```

**Before filing (final version):**
- Remove all confidence labels
- Integrate hedging language into the statement itself
- Preserve credibility by being honest about sources

```
Final affidavit version:

On 8 March 2026, respondent struck affiant on the face. Affiant attended Dr Singh at Medic Centre
the same day. The medical report dated 8 March 2026 (Annexure C) documents bruising consistent
with blunt force trauma.

Respondent told affiant on or about 15 March 2026 that he intended to take the children. Affiant
immediately texted her mother, "R just threatened to take the kids" (copy attached as Annexure D).
Affiant has no other contemporaneous record of this statement.

Affiant recalls that in approximately July 2023, respondent expressed that he did not want affiant
to work. This memory is not supported by contemporaneous records, emails, or witness notes.
```

## 4. Reduce Emotional Narrative

**Rule:** Remove emotional descriptors. Keep observable behaviour and documented impact.

### When to Remove Emotional Language

| Emotional Language | Problem | Factual Replacement |
|---|---|---|
| "I felt terrified" | Subjective; unsupported by context | "Affiant experienced fear and left the residence immediately." OR link to objective evidence: "Affiant's heart rate increased; she left the residence." (if medical evidence available) |
| "He was cruel" | Character judgment; not evidence | Specific behaviour: "Respondent hit affiant on 8 March (Medical report, EV-007)." |
| "This was devastating" | Emotional impact without evidence | Documented impact: "Affiant was unable to work from 10 March to 5 April 2026. GP notes dated 17 March (EV-009) record affiant's report of depression and anxiety." |
| "I was completely isolated" | Hyperbolic; not specific | Documented isolation: "Between March 2023 and July 2024, affiant attended no family events. Police welfare check (10 July 2024, EV-015) noted affiant's report of infrequent social contact." |
| "He was abusive" | Diagnosis; not evidence | Specific behaviours: "Respondent struck affiant (Medical report EV-007); called affiant 'useless' in text messages (EV-031); controlled affiant's location daily (Google Maps access log, EV-045)." |
| "She destroyed our family" | Emotional/rhetorical | Factual sequence: "Affiant left the residence on 12 August 2025. Respondent filed for sole custody on 15 August 2025 (Court document EV-088). A parenting order for supervised contact was made on 3 September 2025 (EV-089)." |
| "I was trapped" | Emotional state without evidence | Behavioural evidence: "Respondent held affiant's keys (Statement of agreed fact, paragraph 8); checked affiant's location via Google Maps daily (Screenshots, EV-045); and required affiant to phone him before leaving the house (SMS exchange, 5 March, EV-019)." |

### Example: Weak Emotional Narrative
*"My relationship was a nightmare. He was so controlling and abusive. I felt terrified all the time. He would make horrible comments about me and my family. I just felt broken. He isolated me completely and I had no one to turn to. The whole experience was utterly devastating."*

**Problems:**
- All subjective feeling; no facts
- No dates, events, or evidence
- "Nightmare," "horrible," "broken," "utterly devastating" erode credibility
- No corroboration possible
- Reads like complaint, not evidence

### Example: Strong Factual Account
*"From January 2022 until August 2025, respondent engaged in the following documented behaviours.*

*Control of movement: Respondent accessed affiant's location via Google Maps between November 2024 and March 2025 (Screenshots, Annexure E). Respondent required affiant to notify him before leaving the house (SMS exchange dated 5 March 2026, Annexure F).*

*Financial control: Respondent withdrew $15,000 from joint account on 12 April 2026 without notice (Bank statement, Annexure G). Affiant's rent cheque bounced the same day.*

*Derogatory communications: Between March and July 2025, respondent sent 47 text messages using derogatory language (SMS thread, Annexure H).*

*Social isolation: Affiant attended no family events from March 2023 to July 2024. Police welfare check dated 10 July 2024 (Annexure I) noted affiant's report of infrequent social contact.*

*Health impact: Affiant was unable to work between 10 March and 5 April 2026. GP notes dated 17 March 2026 (Annexure J) record affiant's report of depression and anxiety. Affiant was prescribed escitalopram (Prescription record, Annexure K).*"

**Strengths:**
- Chronological structure with dates
- Specific behaviours (control, financial, communications, isolation)
- All claims evidenced with annexure references
- Observable facts, not feelings
- Corroboration clear (medical, police, bank, communications)
- Court can assess pattern and impact objectively

## 5. Preserve Legally Relevant Behavioural Patterns

**Rule:** Only assert pattern if supported by 3+ documented instances. Each instance must be dated and sourced.

### Pattern Threshold

A pattern requires:
- **Minimum 3 instances** (single or double incidents do not establish pattern)
- **Timespan:** all instances within defined period (e.g. "over 4 months," "during 18-month period")
- **Each instance dated and sourced** (EV-ID, communication log, police report, etc.)
- **Common theme** (same type of behaviour, same target, or same intent)

### Example: Weak Pattern Claim
*"Respondent was controlling throughout our relationship. He always tried to stop me from seeing my family."*

**Problems:**
- "Always" unsupported by specific instances
- "Controlling" undefined
- No dates or evidence
- No timeframe
- Not falsifiable

### Example: Strong Pattern with Evidence
*"Between February 2023 and May 2023, respondent placed restrictions on affiant's contact with family members on three documented occasions.*

*Instance 1 (15 February 2023): Respondent refused affiant's request to attend her sister's birthday. SMS exchange (EV-012) shows affiant's request at 14:32 and respondent's refusal at 14:47.*

*Instance 2 (10 March 2023): Respondent stated in person, 'I don't want you going to your mum's appointment.' Affiant's cousin, present in the kitchen, confirms this in an email dated 11 March (EV-013).*

*Instance 3 (27 May 2023): Respondent delivered a household rules document (EV-014) which included the rule, 'No visits to family without my approval.' Respondent signed and dated it.*

*Pattern assessment: Over a 4-month period, respondent imposed three escalating restrictions (direct refusal, in-person statement, written rule). This demonstrates a deliberate pattern of controlling affiant's family contact.*"

**Strengths:**
- Three instances within defined timeframe (4 months)
- Each instance dated and sourced
- Escalation evident (refusal → statement → written rule)
- Corroboration cited (SMS, witness email, signed document)
- Conclusion follows from evidence

## 6. Identify Memory-Based Statements

**Rule:** Flag statements based on memory alone. Use contemporaneous records (SMS, email, photo with metadata) as HIGH confidence.

### Confidence by Source Type

| Source Type | Confidence | Example | Language |
|---|---|---|---|
| Contemporaneous communication (SMS, email, voice memo timestamp) | HIGH | SMS sent to sister immediately after incident: "He just hit me" (12 March 3:15pm, EV-007) | "On 12 March 2026 at 3:15pm, affiant texted her sister, 'He just hit me.'" |
| Medical/Police report filed at time | HIGH | Doctor examined bruising same day incident occurred (Medical report 8 March, EV-009) | "On 8 March 2026, affiant attended Dr Singh. The medical report confirms bruising consistent with blunt force trauma." |
| Recording (audio/video with timestamp) | HIGH | Voice recording of respondent threatening affiant (Timestamped audio file, EV-020) | "A voice recording made on 14 March 2026 at 19:30 captures respondent stating, 'I'll take the children away.'" |
| Contemporaneous note (diary, journal with date) | MEDIUM-HIGH | Affiant's handwritten diary entry dated 15 March: "R said he doesn't want me working" | "Affiant's diary dated 15 March 2026 records, 'R said he doesn't want me working.'" |
| Witness corroboration at time (email, message from witness) | MEDIUM | Friend texts same day: "I saw R grab your arm" (SMS dated 18 March 15:47, EV-025) | "On 18 March 2026, affiant's friend texted at 15:47, 'I saw R grab your arm during the argument.'" |
| Affiant memory with later corroboration | MEDIUM | Affiant recalls June incident; witness confirms in interview months later | "Affiant recalls that in June 2023, respondent stated, 'You can't work.' Affiant's cousin confirmed this in an interview on 5 February 2026 (EV-030), stating she heard respondent say this." |
| Affiant memory only (no contemporaneous record) | LOW | Incident in July 2023; no notes, witness, or record at time | "Affiant recalls that in approximately July 2023, respondent told affiant he did not want her to work. Affiant has no contemporaneous record of this statement." |

### Phrasing for Memory-Based Statements

**When statement is memory-only:**
```
"On or about [date], respondent stated, '[Statement].' Affiant has no contemporaneous record
of this communication (no SMS, email, or witness account)."
```

**When memory is supported by later corroboration:**
```
"Affiant recalls that in approximately [month/year], respondent told affiant, '[Statement].'
Affiant's [witness name] confirmed this in an interview dated [date], stating, '[Corroboration].'
(Interview record, EV-0XX)"
```

**When incident is supported by immediate aftermath evidence:**
```
"On [date], affiant recalls respondent [behaviour]. Immediately after the incident, affiant
contacted [person/service]. [Evidence of contact] (EV-0XX) supports affiant's account that
the incident occurred on this date."
```

## 7. Prevent Rhetorical Escalation

**Rule:** Stick to behaviour, not diagnosis or character. One unsourced allegation per 20 sourced facts maintains credibility.

### Rhetorical Patterns to Avoid

| Rhetorical Escalation | Problem | Factual Alternative |
|---|---|---|
| "Respondent is abusive" | Diagnosis; not evidence | Specific behaviours: "Respondent hit affiant (EV-007); called affiant derogatory names in 47 text messages (EV-031); controlled affiant's location daily (EV-045)." |
| "Respondent is a narcissist" | Character diagnosis; inflammatory | Documented behaviours: "Respondent required affiant to provide phone passwords (Affiant testimony); demanded affiant's location continuously (EV-045); boasted about his superior income (SMS, 5 March, EV-019)." |
| "Respondent is controlling" | Vague character claim | Pattern of control: "Respondent placed three documented restrictions on affiant's family contact between February and May 2023 (EV-012, EV-013, EV-014)." |
| "Respondent deliberately tried to destroy me" | Rhetorical escalation; motive claim without evidence | Sequence of documented actions: "Respondent withdrew $15,000 from joint account (Bank statement, EV-020); affiant's rent cheque bounced same day; respondent filed for sole custody three days later (Court document, EV-021)." |
| Accumulation of unsourced adjectives: "abusive, controlling, manipulative, cruel, selfish" | Credibility eroded by unsourced claims | Replace with 1–2 specific behaviours + sources: "Respondent struck affiant on 8 March (Medical report, EV-007) and subsequently controlled affiant's location via Google Maps (Screenshots, EV-045)." |

### Credibility Threshold

**Unsourced allegations per sourced facts:**
- For every unsourced or memory-only allegation, provide 20+ sourced, documented facts
- Example: If claiming "respondent was controlling" (unsourced), support with 20+ documented control instances

| Ratio | Impact | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 unsourced : 30+ sourced | Credible | One memory-based allegation; 35 documented incidents. Court accepts memory claim as plausible. |
| 1 unsourced : 15 sourced | Cautious | One memory-based allegation; 15 documented incidents. Court notes allegation but treats with caution. |
| 1 unsourced : 5 sourced | Weak | One memory-based allegation; only 5 documented facts. Court likely disregards memory claim. |
| Multiple unsourced : Few sourced | Rejected | Affidavit contains 8 memory-only allegations but only 6 documented facts. Court questions affiant's reliability. |

**Example of unsourced accumulation (weak):**
*"Respondent was abusive, controlling, manipulative, and cruel. He isolated me from my family. He was financially coercive. He was intentionally trying to break me down."*

**Count:** 7 unsourced allegations; no documented facts = Instantly rejected

**Rewritten (strong):**
*"Between February 2023 and May 2023, respondent placed restrictions on affiant's family contact. On 15 February, respondent refused affiant's request to attend her sister's birthday (SMS, EV-012). On 10 March, respondent stated, 'I don't want you going to your mum's appointment' (Witness email, EV-013). On 27 May, respondent issued a household rules document prohibiting family visits without approval (EV-014). Between March and July 2025, respondent sent 47 text messages using derogatory language including 'You're stupid,' 'That's pathetic,' and 'You're useless' (SMS thread, EV-031). On 12 April 2026, respondent withdrew $15,000 from the joint account without notice, causing affiant's rent cheque to bounce (Bank statement, EV-020). These documented behaviours demonstrate a pattern of family isolation, derogatory communication, and financial restriction."*

**Count:** 22 documented facts; 0 unsourced allegations; pattern assertion supported = Credible

## 8. Keep Documents Readable to Magistrates

**Rule:** Short sentences, short paragraphs, numbered structure, chronological flow, clear definitions.

### Readability Standards

**Sentence length:** 10–15 words (maximum 20)
- Aim: One idea per sentence
- Avoid: Nested clauses, dependent phrases, "and...and...and" chains

**Paragraph length:** 3–4 sentences (maximum 5)
- Each paragraph covers one idea or one incident
- White space improves comprehension

**Paragraph numbering:** All affidavit paragraphs numbered (1, 2, 3, etc.)
- Easier for judge to cross-reference
- Easier for counsel to cite during hearing

**Chronological structure:** Events ordered by date
- Opens with earliest event
- Builds towards current claim
- No flashbacks or forward references

**Active voice:** Preferred over passive
- "Respondent hit affiant" (active, clear actor)
- Avoid: "Affiant was hit" (passive, obscures respondent's action)

**Define terms at first use:**
- "affiant" = the person swearing the affidavit
- "respondent" = the other party
- "de facto relationship" = defined if relevant
- Acronyms spelled out first time: "DFES (Department for Child Safety)"

### Example: Poor Readability

*"Respondent, who had been my partner for 3.5 years, was, despite what he claimed in his own subsequent statement, someone who, from the very beginning of our relationship in January 2022, engaged in behaviours that were increasingly controlling, abusive, and designed, I believe, to undermine my confidence and isolate me from everyone I cared about, and this escalated significantly when, in March 2023, he forbade me from visiting my family and, when I tried to go anyway, threatened me in ways that were really frightening and made me feel as though I had no choice but to give in to his demands."*

**Problems:**
- 121 words; single sentence (too long)
- Multiple clauses; unclear structure
- Emotional language ("frightening," "really," "no choice")
- No dates except "January 2022" and "March 2023"
- No evidence citations
- No paragraph number
- Passive construction ("was someone who... engaged")

### Example: High Readability

*"5. Affiant and respondent were in a de facto relationship from January 2022 to August 2025. This paragraph describes respondent's pattern of restricting affiant's family contact from March 2023 onwards.*

*6. On 15 February 2023, affiant requested to attend her sister's birthday party. Respondent refused. SMS messages show affiant's request at 14:32 and respondent's refusal at 14:47 (Annexure A).*

*7. On 10 March 2023, affiant asked to attend her mother's medical appointment. Respondent stated, 'I don't want you going to your mum's appointment.' Affiant's cousin was present and confirms this in an email dated 11 March 2023 (Annexure B).*

*8. On 27 May 2023, respondent provided affiant with a written household rules document. Rule 3 stated, 'No visits to family without my approval.' Respondent signed and dated this document (Annexure C).*

*9. Between March 2023 and July 2024, affiant attended no family events. On 10 July 2024, police attended affiant's residence for a welfare check. The police report notes affiant's statement that she had 'infrequent social contact' (Annexure D)."*

**Strengths:**
- Paragraph 5 is topic sentence; clear scope (3.5-year relationship; focus on family restriction from March 2023)
- Paragraphs 6–9 are numbered
- Each paragraph = one incident
- Chronological (15 Feb → 10 Mar → 27 May → Mar–Jul → 10 Jul)
- Sentences 10–15 words
- Specific dates and times (14:32, 14:47)
- All evidence cited (Annexures A, B, C, D)
- Active voice throughout
- No emotional language
- Magistrate can follow easily; can cite "paragraph 7" in judgment

### Formatting Checklist for Court Readiness

- [ ] All paragraphs numbered (1, 2, 3, etc.)
- [ ] All paragraphs 3–5 sentences
- [ ] All sentences 10–20 words
- [ ] Chronological order maintained (earliest to latest)
- [ ] All claims supported by evidence (Annexure or EV-ID cited)
- [ ] No emotional language ("felt," "devastated," "terrified")
- [ ] No unsourced character judgments ("abusive," "manipulative," "controlling")
- [ ] Active voice used throughout
- [ ] Terms defined at first use (affiant, respondent, de facto, acronyms)
- [ ] Direct quotes formatted consistently (in quotation marks with date and source)
- [ ] All Annexures listed in Annexure Index
- [ ] Print sample checked (margins, font, spacing, Bates numbers)
