## Making a Complaint

### Who Can Make a Complaint

Any person affected by teacher conduct can lodge a complaint:
- Parents and guardians
- Students (including minors, with support person assistance)
- Colleagues and other staff
- Members of the public
- Anonymous complainants (accepted but may limit resolution)

### What a Complaint Should Contain

- Complainant's name and contact details (or reason for seeking anonymity)
- Clear description of the concern or incident(s)
- Date(s), time(s), and location(s) of incident(s)
- Names of people involved and any witnesses
- Details of any previous attempts to resolve the matter
- How the conduct affected the complainant, student, or others
- What outcome the complainant is seeking
- Any supporting documentation or evidence

### Anonymous Complaints

Accepted by the Department of Education, TRBWA, and Ombudsman WA. However:
- May be difficult to resolve without follow-up information
- TRBWA may proceed if sufficient information is provided independently
- Ombudsman may be limited in investigation scope
- The identity of the complainant may become apparent through the content of the complaint

## Complaint Pathways by Body

### Level 1: School (Local Resolution)

- Contact the class teacher, year coordinator, or school administration first
- Escalate to the school principal if unsatisfied with initial response
- Department of Education aims to resolve complaints within **14 days** at this stage
- Acknowledgement within **2 working days**

### Level 2: Department of Education — Regional Office

If the school does not satisfactorily resolve the complaint, or if the matter involves the principal's conduct:
- Contact the Education Regional Office for your area
- Complaints Advice Line: **1800 655 985**
- Regional office may refer matters back to school unless there is reason preventing school management

### Level 3: Standards and Integrity Directorate

For allegations of staff misconduct, especially:
- Sexual offences, sexual misconduct, or physical assault on/in presence of a child
- Significant neglect of a child
- Behaviour causing significant emotional or psychological harm

These matters **must be reported to Standards and Integrity without delay** — not deferred until school-level resolution is exhausted.

### Level 4: TRBWA

The independent regulator for teacher professional conduct.

**Who must notify TRBWA:**
- **Section 38:** Teachers must self-notify when legal action is taken against them
- **Section 39:** Teachers must notify if they lose teaching qualifications
- **Section 40:** Teachers must notify if issued with a WWC negative notice
- **Section 41:** Commissioner of Police must notify TRBWA if a registered teacher is prosecuted
- **Section 41B:** Director of Public Prosecutions must notify TRBWA of prosecutions
- **Section 42:** Employers must notify TRBWA within **7 days** if teacher ceases teaching due to serious misconduct (s 48B) or serious incompetence (s 48A)

**Public complaint (s 51):** Any person may make a written complaint to TRBWA about a teacher's conduct. No fee. No formal requirements beyond written form with sufficient detail.

**TRBWA Investigation Process:**
1. Complaint assessed by Board (or referred to Disciplinary Committee)
2. Teacher notified of complaint with full details
3. Initial assessment determines if matter proceeds
4. If serious, referred to Disciplinary Committee or SAT
5. Teacher has right to legal representation
6. Standard of proof: balance of probabilities

### Level 5: External Oversight Agencies

| Body | Jurisdiction | Contact |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **WA Ombudsman** | Investigates decisions and conduct of government agencies including Department of Education; Reportable Conduct Scheme | www.ombudsman.wa.gov.au; 08 9220 7471 |
| **WA Police Force** | Criminal matters or child safety concerns | 131 444 (non-emergency); 000 (emergency) |
| **Commissioner for Children and Young People WA** | Child protection and advocacy | www.ccyp.wa.gov.au |
| **Corruption and Crime Commission (CCC)** | Serious misconduct in WA public sector | www.ccc.wa.gov.au |

### Public Interest Disclosure (Whistleblower Protection)

Under the **Public Interest Disclosure Act 2003 (WA)**:
- Available for reporting teacher misconduct, especially where internal reporting has failed or the complainant fears reprisal
- Confidentiality strictly enforced — breach of confidentiality is a criminal offence
- Protects the discloser from civil liability, criminal liability, and employment action
- Disclosure can be made to: the Ombudsman, the CCC, or the Public Sector Commissioner

## Evidence Gathering and Preservation

### Types of Evidence Accepted

**Documentary evidence:**
- Written records (incident logs, emails, messages, letters)
- Photographs or video recordings (if legally obtained)
- Official school records (obtained via Freedom of Information)
- Medical records or psychological assessments
- Incident reports filed at the time of the event
- Contemporaneous notes (notes written at or close to the time of the incident — carry high evidentiary weight)

**Digital evidence:**
- Email correspondence (with full headers and metadata)
- Text/SMS messages
- Instant messaging (WhatsApp, Teams, etc.)
- Social media screenshots (with URLs, dates, and author visible)
- Learning management system logs (e.g., Connect, Compass)

**Witness statements:**
- Written statements from credible witnesses
- Signed and dated declarations
- Should include what the witness directly observed (not hearsay)
- Multiple independent witnesses to the same incident strengthen evidence significantly

**Physical evidence:**
- Objects related to the incident
- Physical injury documentation (medical records, photographs)
- Clothing or items relevant to the allegation

### How to Preserve Digital Evidence

**Screenshots:**
- Use device's screenshot function (not a photograph of a screen)
- Include full date/time metadata
- Capture full context: sender, recipient, timestamps, platform identifiers
- Save in multiple formats (PDF preferred for permanence, image file as backup)
- Preserve original files without alteration

**Emails and messages:**
- Export to PDF with full headers visible (sender, recipient, date/time, subject)
- Do not forward repeatedly (risks losing metadata)
- Save attachments separately
- For email chains, capture the full thread

**Social media:**
- Screenshots showing full post with date, author, and engagement metrics
- Archive the page using Wayback Machine (archive.org) or equivalent where possible
- Document the URL and the date of access
- Be aware that content may be deleted — preserve promptly

**Chain of custody:**
- Record who discovered the evidence and when
- Document any handling, transfer, or copying of evidence
- Maintain consistent, secure storage
- Avoid contamination or alteration
- Provide a chronological narrative of preservation steps

### Admissibility Standards

**TRBWA/Disciplinary Committee proceedings:**

The Committee may apply evidence-law principles by analogy, but is **not strictly bound** by the rules of evidence (s 120, Teacher Registration Act 2012). Verify the current WA evidence legislation and commencement position before citing any Evidence Act provision. Procedural fairness must be observed.

**Key principles:**
- **Relevance:** Evidence must be relevant to prove or disprove a fact in issue
- **Authenticity:** Original documents or certified copies preferred; digital evidence must establish genuine provenance
- **Hearsay:** Generally inadmissible unless an exception applies (e.g., business records, res gestae, statements in course of duty)
- **Character evidence:** Limited; focus is on conduct, not general character
- **Expert evidence:** Opinion evidence only from qualified experts

**Standard of proof — balance of probabilities:**
- The allegation must be shown to be "more likely than not" to have occurred
- Lower threshold than the criminal standard of "beyond reasonable doubt"
- Pattern of behaviour or multiple lesser incidents may collectively meet the standard (*Briginshaw v Briginshaw* [1938] HCA 34 — the more serious the allegation, the more cogent the evidence required)

**Admissibility pitfalls:**
- Illegally obtained recordings (breach of Surveillance Devices Act 1998) — may be excluded
- Hearsay without exception ("X told me that Y happened")
- Speculation or opinion on matters requiring expert evidence
- Irrelevant character attacks on the complainant
- Evidence obtained in breach of procedural fairness

## Recording Conversations

### Surveillance Devices Act 1998 (WA)

**One-party consent rule (Section 5):**
- **Legal:** To record a private conversation to which you are a party, if you consent (s 5(3))
- The recording must be **reasonably necessary to protect your lawful interests**
- "Lawful interests" includes protecting against harassment, threats, or abusive behaviour (*Farris v Boase* [2013] WASC 227)

**Two-party consent rule:**
- **Illegal** to record a private conversation to which you are **not** a party without consent of all parties
- Illegal to install a hidden listening device to record others' conversations

**Publication restrictions (Section 9):**
- Even if a recording is made legally, **publication or sharing remains restricted**
- Cannot communicate a private conversation obtained via a surveillance device without consent of all principal parties
- Exception: court order under s 31 (in public interest)

**Practical implications for misconduct complaints:**
- A parent **can** record their own conversation with a teacher to protect their lawful interests
- A parent **cannot** secretly record conversations between the teacher and other people
- A student over 14 can arguably consent to recording their own conversation, but this is legally uncertain for younger children
- **Publication of recordings is severely restricted** — sharing with TRBWA or in disciplinary proceedings may require a s 31 application

### Covert recording by employers

- Department of Education investigation interviews are recorded with the teacher's knowledge (using digital voice recorders)
- Covert recording by the employer without the teacher's knowledge is generally **not** permitted under the Surveillance Devices Act

## Student Evidence

### Child-Sensitive Interview Requirements

- Students may have a support person present when making a complaint
- Interviews with children should follow best-practice **trauma-informed** protocols
- Video-recorded interviews are preferable to repeated interviews (especially for young children)
- Children should not be required to give formal evidence without appropriate accommodations (screens, intermediaries, closed hearings)

### TRBWA/SAT Considerations for Child Evidence

- Child evidence is admissible, subject to Evidence Act protections
- Tribunal may grant applications for protective measures (closed hearings, intermediaries)
- Child witness credibility is assessed on age, maturity, and ability to understand questions
- Corroboration is **not legally required** but strengthens the weight of evidence
- A child's account given close to the time of the event (contemporaneous statement) carries more weight than a later recollection

## Freedom of Information

### Process (Freedom of Information Act 1992, WA)

1. Submit written request to the Department of Education
2. Clearly identify the documents sought (e.g., "all incident reports involving [teacher name] from January 2023 to present")
3. Specify the relevant time period
4. Department has **45 days** to respond (extendable to 60 days in complex cases)
5. Fees apply but may be waived if the request is in the public interest
6. Templates available on the Department of Education website

### Records Likely to Be Released

- Incident reports
- Discipline records involving the teacher
- Meeting minutes discussing disciplinary matters
- Written correspondence between school and Department
- Investigation findings (with personal information of third parties redacted)

### Likely Exemptions (Preventing Release)

- Personal information about third parties (other students, other staff) — s 23
- Confidential information obtained during legal advice or investigation — s 32
- Information protected by legal professional privilege — s 33
- Internal deliberative documents (draft decisions, policy recommendations) — s 34
- Law enforcement information — s 29

### Practical Tips

- Be as specific as possible about the documents sought — vague requests are harder to process
- Request documents by type and date range rather than asking for "everything"
- If a request is partially refused, you can apply for internal review within 30 days
- Further appeal to the Information Commissioner is available

## Multiple Instances of Misconduct

### How Pattern Behaviour Is Assessed by TRBWA

**Section 48B (Teacher Registration Act 2012)** defines serious misconduct as conduct that falls **significantly short** of the standard expected of a registered teacher. The concept of repeated or persistent conduct is built into the assessment.

**TRBWA assessment approach:**
- Each incident is evaluated on its own merit
- Combined incidents are then assessed for their cumulative effect
- A single severe incident (e.g., physical assault) can constitute serious misconduct without a pattern
- A pattern of minor incidents can aggregate to reach the threshold of serious misconduct

### Aggregation of Complaints

**Same teacher, multiple complainants:**
- Each complaint is investigated separately but reviewed as part of the teacher's overall conduct history
- Multiple complaints from different sources strengthen the case (reduces the risk of a single vindictive/unreliable complainant)
- TRBWA may combine complaints into a single investigation or disciplinary proceeding
- The Disciplinary Committee may consider all complaints together when assessing the threshold

**Sequential notifications:**
- If an employer becomes aware of repeated serious misconduct, **each instance must be notified to TRBWA separately** (s 42)
- Each notification triggers a separate TRBWA assessment
- A series of notifications creates a documented pattern for disciplinary proceedings

### How Minor Incidents Escalate to Serious Misconduct

**Escalation mechanisms:**

1. **Frequency and persistence:**
   - Single boundary breach → isolated incident (may not meet serious misconduct threshold)
   - Repeated boundary breaches toward the same student(s) → pattern → serious misconduct
   - Multiple victims of similar conduct → systematic behaviour → stronger serious misconduct finding

2. **Severity progression:**
   - Minor professionalism lapse + minor boundary breach + repeated unprofessional conduct → aggregated serious misconduct
   - Each incident need not be individually serious; the combined effect must fall "significantly short" of the expected standard

3. **Failure to respond to warnings:**
   - Single incident with remedial action/counselling → isolated misconduct
   - Repeated incident after formal warning → demonstrates defiance or refusal to comply → escalates seriousness
   - Non-compliance with conditions imposed after earlier incident → disciplinary matter under s 47(b)

4. **Cross-school patterns:**
   - Incidents across multiple schools (e.g., teacher transferred and reoffends) demonstrate entrenched behaviour
   - Historical allegations that were not formally investigated may become relevant if a pattern emerges

### Previous Findings and Their Weight

**Admissibility of prior findings:**
- Previous TRBWA findings against the same teacher are admissible in new disciplinary proceedings
- Previous employer disciplinary outcomes (warnings, reprimands) are admissible as evidence of notice and pattern
- Prior findings are not determinative but carry **significant weight**
- Character and prior history are relevant to pattern analysis and likelihood of rehabilitation

**Example application:**
A teacher with a prior finding of boundary breach involving Student A is now alleged to have breached boundaries with Student B in a similar manner. The prior finding is admissible to:
- Establish pattern of conduct
- Undermine the teacher's credibility if denying the current allegation
- Demonstrate the teacher was on notice that the behaviour was unacceptable
- Support a more severe sanction (the teacher had the opportunity to correct behaviour and did not)

**Deregistration considerations:**
- Multiple serious misconduct findings over time → TRBWA much more likely to cancel registration
- A single finding may result in conditions or suspension
- Repeated findings typically result in suspension or cancellation with disqualification

### Corroboration and Pattern Evidence

**Corroboration:**
- Not legally required for a finding, but strengthens the case
- Multiple independent witnesses to the same incident significantly strengthen evidence
- Contemporaneous documentary evidence corroborates oral testimony
- Medical/psychological records may corroborate harm claims

**Pattern (tendency) evidence:**
- Multiple incidents, even if individually minor, can establish a tendency toward misconduct
- Earlier incidents may be admissible to show pattern, even if not separately actionable
- Pattern evidence shifts focus from isolated incident to systematic/habitual behaviour
- Verify the current WA evidence legislation before citing a tendency-evidence provision; the practical point is to identify probative pattern evidence and assess prejudice/fairness.

## Systemic Failure

### Employer Liability for Failures to Act

**Department of Education/school obligations:**
- Investigate complaints thoroughly and promptly
- Notify TRBWA within 7 days where required (s 42)
- Failure to notify is itself an offence (penalty: $5,000 fine)
- Maintain documented responses to complaints and evidence of investigation

**Systemic failure indicators:**
- Pattern of complaints known to school but not referred to TRBWA
- Repeated incidents after informal resolution without formal investigation
- Transfer of problematic teacher to different school without disclosure ("passing the parcel")
- Failure to implement recommended safeguards after a prior incident
- Inadequate supervision or support for a teacher with known conduct/performance issues
- Failure to conduct adequate reference checks on incoming teachers

### Impact on Disciplinary Proceedings

- Evidence of systemic failure strengthens the complaint (shows the problem was institutional, not isolated)
- May support an allegation that the teacher's conduct was more serious than it appears in isolation (because the school's failure to act enabled escalation)
- The Ombudsman may investigate the school/Department separately for institutional failures
- The CCC may investigate if the failure involves corruption or serious misconduct by school administrators

### Ombudsman Oversight of Systemic Failures

Under the **Parliamentary Commissioner Act 1971 (WA)**, the Ombudsman has jurisdiction to investigate:
- Misconduct by Department staff (including teachers and administrators)
- Systemic failures in handling complaints
- Unreasonable decisions or delays in investigation
- Failure to comply with the Reportable Conduct Scheme

The Ombudsman can issue recommendations for systemic improvements and monitor compliance.
