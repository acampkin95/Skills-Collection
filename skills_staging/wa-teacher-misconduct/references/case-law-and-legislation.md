## Finding Decisions

### SAT Published Decisions

The State Administrative Tribunal publishes decisions via:
- **SAT eCourts Portal:** sat.justice.wa.gov.au
- **AustLII (Australasian Legal Information Institute):** www.austlii.edu.au → Western Australia → State Administrative Tribunal
- Citation format: *Party Name* [Year] WASAT [Number]

**Search strategies for teacher misconduct decisions:**
- Search for "Teacher Registration Board" or "TRBWA" as a party
- Search by Act: "Teacher Registration Act 2012"
- Search for key terms: "serious misconduct", "professional boundaries", "registration cancellation"
- Filter by jurisdiction: WA SAT
- Note: Some decisions suppress the teacher's name (published as "XY" or initials)

### Supreme Court of WA

Appeals from SAT on questions of law are heard by the Supreme Court:
- Citation format: *Party Name* [Year] WASC [Number]
- Available on AustLII → Western Australia → Supreme Court

### TRBWA Annual Reports

TRBWA publishes annual reports with statistical summaries of disciplinary activity:
- Number of complaints received, investigated, referred to Disciplinary Committee and SAT
- Types of misconduct found
- Sanctions imposed
- These are publicly available on the TRBWA website (trb.wa.gov.au)

## Key SAT Decisions — Teacher Discipline

### Decisions Establishing Principles

> **Note:** SAT decisions are fact-specific. The principles extracted below represent general trends, not binding rules. Always verify current decisions on AustLII.

**Serious misconduct threshold:**

SAT has consistently held that "serious misconduct" under s 48B requires conduct that:
- Departs from the standard of behaviour reasonably expected of a registered teacher
- The departure must be of a **serious kind** — not merely careless or inadvertent
- Assessed objectively: would a reasonable person consider this conduct to fall significantly short of the expected standard?
- Context matters: the teacher's role, the vulnerability of the students, the school environment

**Pattern evidence in disciplinary proceedings:**

SAT has accepted pattern or tendency evidence in teacher discipline cases:
- Multiple incidents, even individually minor, can be aggregated to establish serious misconduct
- Prior disciplinary findings are admissible as evidence of notice and pattern
- The *Briginshaw v Briginshaw* [1938] HCA 34 principle applies: the more serious the allegation, the more cogent the evidence must be, but the standard remains balance of probabilities

**Professional boundary decisions:**

SAT decisions have addressed:
- Private social media contact with students as a boundary breach
- One-on-one meetings in unobservable locations
- Personal gift-giving to individual students
- Sharing personal emotional content with students
- Physical contact beyond incidental or safety-related touching
- These matters are assessed on a spectrum from inadvertent to predatory

**Cancellation vs lesser sanctions:**

SAT considers the following when determining whether cancellation is warranted:
- Nature and seriousness of the misconduct
- Whether students were directly harmed
- Teacher's insight, remorse, and cooperation
- Likelihood of rehabilitation
- Prior disciplinary history
- Impact on public confidence in the profession
- Proportionality — is cancellation the least restrictive sanction that adequately protects the public?

## Key Legal Principles from Case Law

### Briginshaw Standard

**Source:** *Briginshaw v Briginshaw* [1938] HCA 34

**Principle:** In civil proceedings (including disciplinary proceedings), the standard of proof is the balance of probabilities. However, where the allegation is serious, the evidence must be "clear and cogent" — the tribunal should not lightly make a finding that has grave consequences for the person concerned.

**Application to teacher misconduct:**
- TRBWA and SAT apply the civil standard (balance of probabilities)
- For serious allegations (sexual misconduct, physical abuse, criminal conduct), the evidence must be proportionately stronger
- This does not change the standard — it changes the quality of evidence required to satisfy it
- Uncorroborated hearsay may be insufficient for serious allegations, even though the balance of probabilities standard applies

### Procedural Fairness (Natural Justice)

**Sources:** Common law; State Administrative Tribunal Act 2004; Teacher Registration Act 2012, s 120

**Principles applied in teacher discipline:**
- **Hearing rule:** The teacher must be given a fair opportunity to be heard before any adverse decision
- **Bias rule:** The decision-maker must not have a personal interest in the outcome or a reasonable apprehension of bias
- **Notice:** The teacher must be given full notice of the allegations, the evidence relied upon, and the potential consequences
- **Representation:** The teacher has the right to legal representation at TRBWA and SAT proceedings
- **Reasons:** Decisions must be accompanied by adequate reasons

**Breach of procedural fairness:**
- A breach may invalidate the disciplinary finding
- SAT can set aside a TRBWA decision on procedural fairness grounds
- However, SAT conducts a fresh hearing on the merits, which may cure earlier procedural defects

### Proportionality of Sanctions

**Principle:** The sanction imposed must be proportionate to the misconduct found. Cancellation of registration — the most severe sanction — should be reserved for the most serious cases or where lesser sanctions are inadequate to protect the public.

**Factors in proportionality assessment:**
- Seriousness of the misconduct
- Degree of harm caused or risked
- Teacher's disciplinary history
- Evidence of insight and rehabilitation
- Impact on the teacher's livelihood
- Public interest in maintaining confidence in the profession
- Whether conditions or supervision could adequately address the risk

### Lawful Interests — Recording Conversations

**Source:** *Farris v Boase* [2013] WASC 227

**Principle:** Under s 5(3) of the Surveillance Devices Act 1998, a person may record a private conversation to which they are a party if it is "reasonably necessary to protect their lawful interests." The Supreme Court in *Farris v Boase* confirmed that "lawful interests" includes protecting against harassment, threats, or abusive behaviour — it is not limited to protecting legal rights in the narrow sense.

**Application:**
- A parent recording a conversation with a teacher to document alleged misconduct is likely protected
- The recording must be of a conversation to which the parent is a party (one-party consent)
- Publication of the recording remains restricted under s 9 — sharing with TRBWA or in proceedings may require a s 31 application

## Evidentiary Principles from Case Law

### Tendency Evidence

**Source:** Verify current WA evidence legislation and transitional provisions before citing a section number.

**Principle:** Evidence of a person's character, reputation, conduct, or tendency is admissible if its probative value substantially outweighs any prejudicial effect.

**Application in teacher discipline:**
- Prior boundary breaches are admissible to show a tendency toward boundary violations
- Multiple complaints from different sources about similar conduct strengthen the tendency argument
- Earlier incidents that were not formally actioned may still be admissible as tendency evidence if the pattern is relevant
- The tendency must be specific — general "bad character" evidence is less likely to be admitted

### Corroboration

**Principle:** Corroboration is not legally required for a finding of misconduct on the balance of probabilities. However, it significantly strengthens the evidence.

**Practical application:**
- A single uncorroborated allegation can sustain a finding if the complainant is credible and the evidence is cogent
- Multiple independent witnesses to the same or similar conduct substantially strengthen the case
- Contemporaneous documentary evidence (notes, messages, photographs) corroborates oral testimony
- Medical, psychological, or counselling records may corroborate claims of harm

### Hearsay

**Principle:** Hearsay evidence ("X told me that Y happened") is generally inadmissible unless an exception applies.

**Exceptions commonly relevant in teacher discipline:**
- **Business records:** Records created in the ordinary course of business (incident reports, school records)
- **Res gestae:** Statements made contemporaneously with the event, while under the stress of the occasion
- **Statements in course of duty:** Statements made by a person performing their duties at the time
- **Prior consistent statements:** Statements made by the complainant shortly after the event, consistent with their testimony (relevant to credibility, not proof of the facts)

**TRBWA flexibility:** The Disciplinary Committee is not strictly bound by the rules of evidence (s 120, Teacher Registration Act 2012). It may receive hearsay evidence but must assign appropriate weight. Hearsay alone is unlikely to sustain a serious misconduct finding.

### Expert Evidence

**Principle:** Opinion evidence is only admissible from witnesses qualified as experts in their field.

**Common expert evidence in teacher misconduct:**
- Psychological assessments (fitness to teach, risk of reoffending, impact on student)
- Medical evidence (injuries, substance abuse)
- Digital forensic evidence (authentication of digital communications)
- Educational standards experts (whether conduct departed from professional norms)

## Interstate and National Authorities

### National Teacher Registration Framework

While each state and territory has its own teacher registration body, the **Australian Institute for Teaching and School Leadership (AITSL)** coordinates national standards:
- **Australian Professional Standards for Teachers (APST)** — adopted by TRBWA under s 20 of the Teacher Registration Act
- **National Teacher Registration Agreement** — mutual recognition of registration across jurisdictions

### Cross-Jurisdictional Misconduct

**Mutual notification:**
- State and territory teacher registration bodies share information about disciplinary findings
- A teacher deregistered in one state cannot simply register in another without disclosure
- Interstate disciplinary findings are relevant to the WA fit and proper person assessment (s 24)
- The National Teacher Registration Agreement facilitates information sharing

**Teacher mobility and "jurisdiction shopping":**
- A teacher who loses registration in WA and attempts to register elsewhere will face disclosure obligations
- Interstate bodies will check with TRBWA as part of their assessment
- The 2022 WWC amendments further strengthened cross-jurisdictional information sharing for child safety

### Persuasive Authorities from Other Jurisdictions

Interstate decisions are not binding on TRBWA or SAT but may be cited as persuasive authority, particularly where:
- The legislative framework is analogous
- The factual scenario is comparable
- WA has no directly on-point decision
- The interstate tribunal's reasoning is well-developed

Common areas where interstate decisions are cited:
- The nexus test for out-of-school conduct
- Professional boundary breach thresholds
- Proportionality of sanctions for similar misconduct types
- Grooming behaviour assessment frameworks
