## 2024 Amendment Status and Reforms

Primary source: **Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024 (WA), No. 11 of 2024**, assented to **22 April 2024**.

Commencement:

- Part 1 commenced on assent: **22 April 2024**.
- First reform tranche commenced on **16 May 2024** under the Residential Tenancies Amendment Act 2024 Commencement Proclamation 2024.
- Rent increase and pets/minor modification provisions commenced on **29 July 2024** under Commencement Proclamation (No. 2) 2024.
- The remainder commenced on **28 March 2026** under Commencement Proclamation 2026. As at **9 May 2026**, the 2024 Amendment Act is fully commenced.

Key practical reforms:

- **Rent bidding**: residential tenancies must be advertised or offered at a fixed rent, unless rent is calculated by reference to tenant income. Landlords and agents must not solicit offers above the advertised rent (RTA s 27AA).
- **Rent increases**: rent increases are generally limited to once every **12 months** rather than every 6 months (RTA ss 30, 31A, 31B; transitional ss 99-100).
- **Retaliatory action**: tenants may seek court relief where landlord action is likely retaliatory, including certain breach notices, rent increases, termination action, or refusal to renew after tenant enforcement activity or complaints (RTA ss 26A-26B).
- **Pets**: tenants may keep pets with landlord consent; assistance animals do not require landlord consent. Refusal and non-standard conditions are limited and can go to the Commissioner (RTA ss 50A-50I).
- **Modifications**: new standard terms cover furniture safety modifications, family-violence security modifications, minor modifications, major modifications, restoration duties, and disability-access protections (RTA ss 50J-50ZE).
- **Bond release**: Part 5A creates a direct bond-release application process through the Bond Administrator, with unresolved applications referred to the Commissioner and appeal/court pathways preserved (RTA ss 81C-81T).
- **Approved forms**: many prescribed/Minister-approved forms were replaced by the "approved form" framework (RTA s 88C). Always verify the current Consumer Protection form before serving a notice or application.

## Lease Types

### Fixed-Term Lease (s 7 RTA)

- **Duration**: Agreed start and end date in writing
- **Minimum**: No statutory minimum (market practice usually 6 or 12 months)
- **Maximum**: No statutory maximum
- **Form**: Must be in writing (s 7(1)). If not in writing, provisions still apply but landlord risks penalties
- **Required contents**: Names, address for service, rent amount and frequency, bond amount, term dates, any special terms
- **Ending**: Automatically ends on the expiry date. Either party may give notice earlier per statutory grounds
- **Conversion**: If tenant stays past expiry without new agreement, converts to **periodic tenancy** on the same terms (s 56)
- **Break lease (tenant)**: Tenant may end early only under the agreement, by negotiated surrender, by statutory/court pathway, or by accepting liability for the landlord's reasonable loss. Do not assume a general 14-day no-grounds break right for fixed terms

### Periodic Tenancy (s 8 RTA)

- **No fixed end date** — continues until either party gives valid notice
- **Can arise from**: expired fixed-term without renewal, or original agreement stating periodic
- **Notice to end (tenant)**: **21 days written notice** (s 58(1)(a))
- **Notice to end (landlord without grounds)**: **60 days written notice** (s 58(1)(b))
- **Notice to end (landlord with grounds)**: Varies by ground — see Termination section

### Key Differences

| Feature | Fixed-Term | Periodic |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Duration | Agreed dates | Indefinite |
| Rent increase | Per lease/statutory rules; usually 60 days notice and 12-month frequency limit | 60 days written notice and 12-month frequency limit |
| Tenant ends early | Break lease (compensation) | 21 days notice |
| Landlord ends without grounds | Cannot — must wait for expiry | 60 days notice |
| Security | Higher for both parties | Flexible, less security |
| Conversion on expiry | Becomes periodic | N/A |

## Key Rights and Obligations

### Landlord Obligations (s 42 RTA)

1. Provide vacant possession at start
2. Maintain premises in reasonable repair
3. Ensure premises are reasonably clean at start
4. Comply with all building, health, and safety laws
5. Respect tenant's quiet enjoyment (s 38)
6. Not interfere with supply of gas, electricity, water (s 49)
7. Provide and maintain locks (s 44)
8. Give proper notice before entry

### Tenant Obligations (s 43 RTA)

1. Pay rent on time
2. Keep premises reasonably clean
3. Not cause nuisance or interference with neighbours
4. Not use premises for illegal purpose
5. Not maliciously damage premises
6. Report damage or need for repairs
7. Comply with strata by-laws (if applicable)
8. Return premises in substantially same condition at end (fair wear and tear excepted)

### Quiet Enjoyment (s 38)

Tenant has the right to peaceful enjoyment without unreasonable interference. Breach includes:
- Unlawful entry
- Harassment or intimidation
- Persistent unreasonably early or late contact
- Shutting off utilities

## Bonds

- **Maximum bond**: 4 weeks rent (unfurnished) or 6 weeks rent (if rent exceeds $1,200/week, furnished properties may differ — check current limits)
- **Pet bond**: If the tenant is permitted to keep a pet, the landlord may take the prescribed pet bond amount for pet damage or fumigation costs. Assistance animals are not pets for this purpose
- **Lodgement**: Landlord/property manager must lodge the bond with the **Bond Administrator** (Consumer Protection) within **14 days** using the approved process (s 29)
- **Condition report**: Must be completed at start and end (Form 1 Condition Report) — both parties sign
- **Bond release**: Since **28 March 2026**, one or more parties can apply to the Bond Administrator for full or partial release after termination/vacant possession. If all known parties agree, the bond is paid as agreed; if disputed or not answered, the application is referred to the Commissioner. Court orders still control where made (Part 5A)
- **Penalty for failing to lodge**: Up to $5,000 (s 29(3))

### Bond Disputes

1. Confirm whether the bond is held by the Bond Administrator and whether a Part 5A security bond release application has been made.
2. If served with a bond-release notice, respond by the stated notice-period deadline if disputing the proposed distribution.
3. The Commissioner decides referred bond-release applications by deciding any landlord entitlement under s 81E, then distributing the balance.
4. The Magistrates Court can still decide bond disputes or make bond orders, including when the Commissioner declines to decide or a party appeals.
5. Evidence usually includes condition reports, dated photos, invoices/quotes, rent ledgers, notices, and correspondence.

## Rent Increases

### During Fixed-Term

- Only if the lease **specifically provides** for increases (amount, method, or frequency)
- **Minimum 60 days written notice** stating new amount and date it takes effect (s 46)
- Cannot increase more frequently than every **12 months**, subject to transitional rules for fixed-term agreements entered, renewed, or extended before the relevant commencement

### During Periodic Tenancy

- **Minimum 60 days written notice** (s 46)
- Cannot increase more frequently than every **12 months**
- Tenant may dispute if increase is excessive — apply to Magistrates Court within 30 days of notice

### Advertising and Rent Bidding

- Residential premises must be advertised or offered at a **fixed rent amount**, unless rent is calculated by reference to the tenant's income (s 27AA).
- Landlords and agents must not solicit or invite offers above the advertised rent.
- A tenant may still need advice about whether voluntarily offering more rent creates a different issue; do not treat every above-advertised offer as landlord solicitation without evidence.

### Excessive Rent

Tenant can apply to Magistrates Court arguing the increase is excessive compared to:
- Market rent for comparable properties
- State of repair
- Terms of the tenancy
- Since the last increase

## Pets and Assistance Animals

### Pets

- A tenant may keep a pet at the premises only with landlord consent (s 50A).
- A request must be made in the approved form. The landlord must respond in writing within **14 days** (s 50B).
- If the landlord does not respond within 14 days and does not apply to the Commissioner for approval to refuse within that time, consent is taken to be given.
- Landlord consent may include reasonable conditions about the number of animals, cleaning, maintenance, fumigation, prescribed matters, or conditions approved by the Commissioner (s 50C).
- Refusal is limited to statutory grounds: breach of written law, local law, strata/community title scheme by-laws, Commissioner approval, or a prescribed ground (s 50D).
- The Commissioner may approve refusal where, for example, the premises are unsuitable, the number of pets would be unreasonable, damage risk cannot realistically be covered by the bond, health/safety risk is unacceptable, or the landlord would suffer undue hardship (s 50E).
- The tenant remains responsible for nuisance and pet damage. Pet damage is not fair wear and tear (s 50I).

### Assistance Animals

- Assistance animals are not "pets" under the amended RTA definition.
- A tenant may keep an assistance animal without landlord consent, but written laws, local laws, and strata/community title scheme by-laws may still need checking.

## Tenant Modifications

### Furniture Safety Modifications

- A tenant may request consent to attach furniture, or a thing to attach furniture, to a wall for the safety of a child or person with disability (ss 50J-50L).
- The request must be in the approved form and describe the modification.
- The landlord must respond within **14 days**. If they do not respond, consent is taken to be given.
- Refusal is limited, including asbestos disturbance, heritage listing, scheme by-laws, or prescribed grounds. Disability-access refusals may be prohibited.

### Family Violence Security Modifications

- A tenant may make prescribed modifications without landlord consent where necessary to prevent entry by a person whose interest has been terminated for family violence reasons, or where the tenant reasonably suspects family violence risk (s 50M).
- The tenant must give written notice describing the modification, use a suitable tradesperson, and provide the invoice within 14 days after completion.
- This pathway does not apply to premises entered in the State Register of Heritage Places.

### Minor Modifications

- A tenant may request consent for a prescribed minor modification in the approved form (ss 50N-50V).
- The landlord must respond in writing within **14 days**.
- If the landlord does not respond and does not apply to the Commissioner for approval to refuse within that period, consent is taken to be given.
- Refusal is limited, including asbestos disturbance, heritage listing, scheme by-laws, written-law inconsistency, Commissioner approval, or prescribed grounds.
- Refusal of a modification reasonably required for disability access may be prohibited under the Equal Opportunity Act 1984 (WA) and Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (Cth).

### Major and Other Modifications

- Major modifications depend on the agreement and landlord consent rules (ss 50W-50Z).
- Requests for major/other modifications use the approved form and generally require a written response within **28 days**.
- Tenants usually bear the cost of making, maintaining, removing, and restoring modifications unless the landlord agrees otherwise.

## Repairs and Maintenance

### Urgent Repairs (s 42(2))

Landlord must attend to **urgently** (typically within 24–48 hours):
- Burst water service or serious water leak
- Blocked or broken toilet
- Serious roof leak
- Gas leak
- Dangerous electrical fault
- Flooding or serious flood damage
- Storm or fire damage
- Faulty heater in winter
- Broken refrigerator (if supplied)
- Any fault making premises unsafe or uninhabitable

### Non-Urgent Repairs

- Tenant must notify landlord in writing
- Landlord must respond within **reasonable time** (typically 14 days)
- If landlord fails to act, tenant may issue **Notice of Breach** (Form 20)

### Tenant Remedies for Failure to Repair

1. **Notice of Breach** (Form 20) — gives landlord **14 days** to remedy
2. If landlord still fails: apply to Magistrates Court for orders
3. Court may order: specific performance, rent reduction, compensation, or termination
4. **Emergency repairs**: Tenant may arrange repairs up to **2 weeks rent** and deduct from rent (s 45) — must have made reasonable attempts to notify landlord first

## Entry and Access (s 39–41)

| Purpose | Notice Required | Frequency Limit |
|---------|----------------|-----------------|
| Routine inspection | 7–14 days written | Max 4 per year |
| Repairs | 72 hours written | As needed |
| Showing to prospective tenant/buyer | 72 hours written | Reasonable |
| Valuation | 7 days written | As needed |
| Emergency | No notice required | N/A |

Entry must be between **8am and 6pm** on a business day unless tenant agrees otherwise.

## Ending a Tenancy

### By Tenant

| Method | Notice | Grounds Required |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| Periodic — without grounds | 21 days written | None |
| Fixed-term — early exit | Agreement, statutory ground, court order, or negotiated surrender | Do not assume a general 14-day no-grounds right; compensation may be payable |
| Fixed-term — expiry | No notice needed (but good practice) | None |
| Landlord breach | 14 days written (Form 21) | Landlord breached RTA |
| Family violence | 7 days written + evidence | s 79A (Family Violence Provisions) |
| Premises destroyed/uninhabitable | Immediate | s 63 |

### By Landlord

| Method | Notice | Grounds Required |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| Periodic — without grounds | **60 days written** | None |
| Fixed-term — no grounds | Cannot end before expiry | N/A |
| Tenant breach | **14 days written** (Form 20 → Form 22) | Rent arrears (14+ days), breach of agreement |
| Sale of property | **60 days written** | Property sold with vacant possession |
| Owner/family moving in | **60 days written** | Genuine intention to reside |
| Demolition/renovation | **60 days written** | Permit obtained, genuine intention |
| Mortgagee possession | Per mortgage terms | Court order or exercise of power of sale |

### Retaliatory Action

If a tenant reasonably believes landlord action is retaliatory, the tenant may apply to a competent court for relief (ss 26A-26B). Relevant landlord action can include:

- issuing certain breach notices other than for rent arrears
- increasing rent
- terminating the agreement
- refusing, at the end of the current agreement, to enter into a further agreement

The protected tenant activity can include enforcing RTA rights, giving a landlord breach notice, seeking urgent-repair reimbursement, applying to court, complaining to Consumer Protection or another government entity, or having a court order in force. The court may set aside the landlord action and/or order compensation.

### Abandonment

If tenant appears to have abandoned:
1. Landlord must make reasonable inquiries (contact tenant, check with neighbours, verify utilities disconnected)
2. Issue **Notice of Abandonment** (Form 8) — 7 days to respond
3. After 7 days with no response: may re-enter and secure premises
4. Apply to Magistrates Court for abandonment order if needed

## Dispute Resolution

### Step 1: Direct Negotiation

- Communicate in writing
- Keep copies of all correspondence
- Note dates, times, and what was agreed

### Step 2: Consumer Protection Conciliation / Commissioner Pathways

- Free service
- Phone: 1300 304 054
- Online complaint via `consumerprotection.wa.gov.au`
- Conciliator contacts both parties, facilitates resolution
- Voluntary — either party can decline
- No binding orders — recommendations only
- Some 2024 reform matters now go to the Commissioner for decision, including pet/refusal-condition disputes, minor-modification disputes, and referred bond-release applications. Check the current approved form and pathway before filing in court.

### Step 3: Magistrates Court Application

- Filing fee applies (check current schedule — typically ~$100 for tenancy disputes)
- **No lawyer required** — either party can self-represent
- Time limits: generally 6 months from the date of the dispute
- Court can make orders for:
  - Payment of money (rent arrears, compensation, bond)
  - Specific performance (repairs, compliance)
  - Termination and possession
  - Injunctions

### Magistrates Court Process

1. File **Application** (Form 6) at the Magistrates Court nearest to the property
2. Court sets a **directions hearing** (usually within 4–6 weeks)
3. At directions hearing: identify issues, set timetable, order conciliation if not already done
4. **Mediation** — court may refer to mediation before hearing
5. **Hearing** — both parties present evidence, witnesses, submissions
6. **Judgment** — magistrate makes orders
7. **Enforcement** — if party does not comply, apply for enforcement (warrant of execution, garnishee order)

### Evidence for Court

- Written tenancy agreement
- Condition reports (entry and exit) with photos
- Bond lodgement receipt
- Rent records / bank statements
- Written notices (breach, termination)
- Correspondence (emails, texts, letters — see `references/sms-imessage-analysis.md`)
- Photos and videos of premises/condition
- Quotes and invoices for repairs
- Witness statements
- Consumer Protection conciliation records

## Lodging and Boarding

**Critical distinction**: Lodging and boarding arrangements are **NOT** covered by the Residential Tenancies Act 1987 (WA). They are governed by common law and contract principles.

### What is a Lodger/Boarder?

- **Lodger**: Occupies a room in the landlord's residence, shares facilities, does not have exclusive possession
- **Boarder**: Same as lodger but also receives meals/services
- **Key test**: Does the occupant have **exclusive possession** of the premises? If yes → tenancy (RTA applies). If no → licence/lodger (RTA does not apply)

### Indicators of Lodging (not tenancy)

- Landlord resides at the property and shares common areas
- Landlord provides services (meals, cleaning, linen)
- No exclusive possession of the premises
- Room can be entered by landlord without notice
- Agreement is a licence, not a lease
- Short-term or casual arrangement

### Indicators of Tenancy (RTA applies)

- Tenant has exclusive possession of the dwelling or self-contained unit
- Separate premises with own entrance
- No shared living with landlord
- Written or oral lease agreement for exclusive possession
- Tenant responsible for own living expenses

### Rights of Lodgers/Boarders

Since the RTA does not apply:
- **No bond protection** under Bond Administrator — bond is held by landlord
- **No minimum notice periods** — governed by the agreement terms
- **No quiet enjoyment statutory right** — but contractual terms may provide
- **No RTA Consumer Protection conciliation pathway** — disputes usually go directly to court unless another statutory or contractual pathway applies
- **Contract law applies** — terms of the agreement are enforceable
- **Australian Consumer Law** may apply to unfair contract terms
- **Equal Opportunity Act 1984 (WA)** — discrimination protections still apply

### Disputes for Lodgers/Boarders

1. Review the written agreement first
2. Attempt direct negotiation
3. If agreement provides for dispute resolution — follow that process
4. Magistrates Court — minor civil claim (claims up to $75,000)
5. Legal Aid WA or community legal centre for advice

## Caravan Parks and Long-Stay Residents

Covered by the **Caravan Parks and Camping Grounds Act 1995 (WA)** and the **Caravan Parks and Camping Grounds Regulations 1997**.

### Key Protections for Long-Stay Residents

- Written park rules must be provided
- 60 days notice for rent increase
- 30 days notice for park closure
- Dispute resolution through Consumer Protection
- Cannot be evicted without proper notice and grounds

## Common Mistakes

- Assuming a lodger/boarder has the same rights as a tenant under the RTA
- Calling the WA RTA the 1995 Act. The principal residential tenancy statute is the **Residential Tenancies Act 1987 (WA)**.
- Not using the current approved form for notices and Commissioner applications
- Failing to lodge bond within 14 days
- Serving insufficient notice periods or using outdated rent-increase frequency rules
- Treating pet or minor-modification consent as purely discretionary after the 2024 reforms
- Missing the Part 5A bond-release response deadline and allowing a dispute to be decided without the party's submissions
- Not completing condition reports at entry and exit with photos
- Self-help eviction (changing locks, cutting utilities, removing possessions) — **illegal** under s 49 RTA
- Confusing Consumer Protection conciliation (voluntary, non-binding), Commissioner decisions, and court orders
- Not mitigating loss in break-lease situations (landlord must make reasonable efforts to re-let)
