## In-School vs Out-of-School Conduct

### General Principle

The Teacher Registration Act 2012 does not limit misconduct to conduct occurring within school hours or on school premises. **Section 48B** (serious misconduct) applies to conduct that falls significantly short of the standard expected of a registered teacher — regardless of where or when it occurs.

The critical question is whether the conduct has a sufficient connection ("nexus") to the teacher's professional role to warrant disciplinary action.

### Conduct Occurring Within School Context

The following is clearly within TRBWA's disciplinary reach:
- Conduct during school hours, on school premises
- Conduct at school-related events (excursions, camps, sports carnivals, after-school activities)
- Conduct involving students in any context (even outside school hours)
- Conduct using school systems or resources (email, LMS, school devices)
- Conduct during professional development or training events
- Conduct at staff social events where a teacher-student or teacher-parent interaction occurs

### Conduct Outside the School Context

Out-of-school conduct is subject to the "nexus test" (see below). Categories of private conduct that have been found to constitute disciplinary matters include:

**Criminal conduct:**
- Any criminal conviction or charge — s 47(d) of the Teacher Registration Act creates an automatic disciplinary matter for serious offences, actionable offences, and prescribed offences
- This applies regardless of whether the offence occurred in a professional context
- The **Spent Convictions Act 1988 (WA)** provides limited protection for old convictions, but **s 6(1)(ba)** excludes child-related employment from the spent convictions regime — meaning spent convictions remain disclosable for teachers

**Substance abuse:**
- Drug offences (possession, supply) — even if not school-related, a drug conviction creates a disciplinary matter under s 47(d)
- Alcohol-related offences (drink driving, public intoxication) may constitute a disciplinary matter if sufficiently serious or repeated, demonstrating unfitness

**Sexual conduct:**
- Sexual relationship with a current or former student (even if the student is over 18) — the power imbalance and breach of trust make this serious misconduct
- Child sexual abuse material offences — automatic disciplinary matter regardless of context
- Sexual offences against any person — assessed under the nexus test

**Domestic and family violence:**
- Family violence restraining orders (VROs) under the **Restraining Orders Act 1997 (WA)** — the existence of a VRO is relevant to the fit and proper person test under s 24
- Criminal convictions arising from family violence incidents
- Pattern of violence may indicate risk to students, even without direct student involvement

**Financial misconduct:**
- Fraud, theft, or dishonesty outside the school context may be relevant if it goes to the teacher's character and fitness

## The Nexus Test

### What Is the Nexus Test?

The nexus test determines whether private conduct is sufficiently connected to the teacher's professional role to justify disciplinary action. It is not a statutory test with a single legislative source — it is a principle developed through case law and applied by TRBWA, SAT, and equivalent bodies nationally.

### Key Factors in the Nexus Assessment

The following factors are considered when determining whether out-of-school conduct has a sufficient nexus to the teaching profession:

1. **Nature of the conduct and its seriousness**
   - More serious conduct (violence, sexual offences, dishonesty) has a stronger nexus
   - Trivial or minor private conduct is unlikely to meet the threshold

2. **Relevance to the teacher's professional role**
   - Conduct involving children (even non-students) has strong nexus
   - Conduct demonstrating unfitness to be responsible for young people
   - Conduct undermining the trust placed in teachers by the community

3. **Impact on the teacher's ability to perform their role**
   - Does the conduct render the teacher unable to fulfil professional obligations?
   - Would students, parents, or colleagues reasonably lose confidence in the teacher?

4. **Impact on the reputation of the profession**
   - Does the conduct bring the teaching profession into disrepute?
   - Would a reasonable member of the public, knowing the facts, consider the teacher unfit?

5. **Whether the conduct was public or became publicly known**
   - Widely publicised conduct has a stronger nexus than truly private matters
   - Media reporting or community awareness increases the reputational impact

6. **Whether the teacher's role was connected to the conduct**
   - Did the teacher use their position, authority, or access to students/children in the conduct?
   - Did the conduct occur in a context where the teacher's professional status was known?

### Application by TRBWA and SAT

TRBWA applies the nexus test pragmatically. In practice:

- **Strong nexus (almost always meets threshold):** Criminal convictions involving children, sexual offences, violence, child sexual abuse material, drug supply
- **Moderate nexus (usually meets threshold):** Repeated DUI, domestic violence with conviction, fraud or dishonesty, drug possession
- **Weak nexus (may not meet threshold):** Minor traffic offences, private disputes between adults with no violence, single minor offence with no child connection
- **No nexus (unlikely to meet threshold):** Purely private consensual adult conduct with no professional connection, minor regulatory infringements

### Relevant Interstate Authorities

While WA has its own regulatory framework, interstate decisions provide persuasive (not binding) guidance:

- Victorian Institute of Teaching v *various* — VIT has consistently found that out-of-school criminal conduct affecting fitness is a disciplinary matter
- NSW NESA — applies a similar nexus test under the Teacher Accreditation Act 2004 (NSW)
- Queensland College of Teachers — applies a "bringing the profession into disrepute" standard

The principle is consistent nationally: private conduct that undermines public confidence in a teacher's fitness to teach, or that demonstrates a risk to student safety, is within the regulator's disciplinary reach.

## Conflicts of Interest

### Legislative and Policy Framework

**Primary legislation:**
- **Public Sector Management Act 1994 (WA)** — s 9 establishes general principles of public administration including integrity and avoidance of conflicts
- **Commissioner's Instruction 7: Code of Ethics** — requires public officers (including teachers in public schools) to act with integrity, avoid conflicts of interest, and disclose potential conflicts

**Department of Education policy:**
- **Conflict of Interest Policy** (current version) — defines conflicts of interest, disclosure requirements, and management strategies
- **Staff Conduct and Discipline Policy** — breach of conflict of interest obligations may constitute misconduct

**TRBWA relevance:**
- A conflict of interest does not automatically constitute serious misconduct under s 48B
- However, failure to declare a conflict, or acting on a conflict to the detriment of students, can escalate to serious misconduct
- Persistent or deliberate conflicts may go to the fit and proper person assessment under s 24

### Types of Conflicts of Interest for Teachers

**Financial conflicts:**
- Receiving payments, gifts, or benefits from parents, suppliers, or contractors
- Personal financial interest in a vendor or service provider used by the school
- Using school resources or position for personal financial gain
- Recommending products or services from which the teacher benefits financially

**Personal relationship conflicts:**
- Teaching or assessing one's own child or a relative's child
- Supervising or managing a family member or close friend on staff
- Romantic relationship with a parent of a student in one's class
- Relationship with a person involved in a complaint or investigation concerning the teacher

**Secondary employment conflicts:**
- Private tutoring of one's own students (see separate section below)
- Working for a competitor or alternative education provider
- Employment that creates scheduling conflicts with teaching duties
- Employment that could reasonably be seen as incompatible with the teacher's professional role

**Procurement and resource conflicts:**
- Involvement in procurement decisions where the teacher has a personal interest
- Allocation of school resources (equipment, funding, opportunities) that benefits the teacher personally
- Selection of textbooks, materials, or excursion providers in which the teacher has an interest

**Assessment conflicts:**
- Assessing or marking work of students with whom the teacher has a personal relationship
- Involvement in academic misconduct decisions for students known personally to the teacher
- Participation in scholarship, award, or selection decisions where a conflict exists

### Disclosure and Management Obligations

**Public school teachers (Department of Education):**
1. **Disclose** any actual, potential, or perceived conflict of interest to their principal or line manager
2. **Document** the conflict in writing
3. **Follow the management plan** determined by the school or Department — which may include:
   - Removal from the conflicted decision
   - Transfer of the student to another class or assessor
   - Divestment of the financial interest
   - Cessation of the secondary employment
4. **Ongoing obligation** — disclose new conflicts as they arise; a one-time disclosure is not sufficient if circumstances change

**Non-government school teachers:**
- Governed by the school's own conflict of interest policy
- TRBWA professional standards apply regardless of school sector
- Serious undisclosed conflicts may still constitute misconduct under s 48B

### Consequences of Undisclosed or Unmanaged Conflicts

| Severity | Likely outcome |
|----------|---------------|
| Minor conflict, promptly disclosed, no harm | Management plan — no disciplinary action |
| Conflict disclosed late but no harm resulted | Counselling, possible written warning |
| Deliberate failure to disclose with potential for harm | Formal disciplinary action; possible TRBWA notification |
| Conflict acted upon to the detriment of students | Serious misconduct (s 48B); TRBWA notification required |
| Financial fraud or corruption arising from conflict | Criminal referral; termination; TRBWA cancellation likely |

## Private Tutoring

### Policy Framework

**Department of Education — Secondary Employment Policy:**
- Teachers in WA public schools must obtain **written approval** before undertaking secondary employment (including private tutoring)
- Approval is assessed against potential conflicts of interest, impact on primary duties, and reputational risk
- The principal or line manager assesses the application
- Approval may be subject to conditions

**Department of Education — Private Tutors in Public Schools Policy:**
- External private tutors may operate on school premises subject to school approval
- Tutors must hold a current WWC Card
- Schools must maintain records of approved tutors
- This policy governs external tutors, not teachers tutoring their own students

### Teacher Tutoring Own Students — The Core Conflict

A WA public school teacher who privately tutors students from their own school faces significant conflict of interest issues:

**The concern:**
- The teacher has a financial incentive to recommend tutoring for students (or to underperform in class to create demand)
- Students who pay for tutoring may receive preferential treatment or better grades
- Students who cannot afford tutoring are disadvantaged
- Parents may feel coerced into paying for tutoring to maintain their child's relationship with the teacher
- The power dynamic between teacher and student is exploited for financial gain

**TRBWA position:**
- Private tutoring of one's own students is a **high-risk conflict of interest**
- It is not automatically prohibited, but it is strongly discouraged and requires full disclosure
- Failure to disclose is likely to constitute a breach of professional standards (Standard 7 — professional engagement)
- If the teacher uses their position to solicit tutoring clients, this is likely serious misconduct

**Best practice (and Department of Education expectation):**
- Do not privately tutor students from your own school (especially your own classes)
- If tutoring students from other schools, obtain written secondary employment approval
- Maintain clear records and boundaries between teaching and tutoring roles
- Never use school resources, systems, or contact details for tutoring business
- Never solicit tutoring clients through the school or school community

### Private Tutoring of Students From Other Schools

This is generally permissible subject to:
1. Obtaining secondary employment approval
2. Holding a current WWC Card (if tutoring minors outside the school context)
3. Maintaining professional boundaries (no inappropriate contact, no sharing of school information)
4. No conflict with primary teaching duties (scheduling, preparation time, fatigue)

### Tutoring After Leaving a School

A teacher who has left a school may tutor former students without the same conflict concerns, provided:
- No current professional relationship exists
- The teacher did not solicit the tutoring arrangement while still employed at the school
- Professional boundaries are maintained (especially with minors)
- WWC Card obligations are met

### Disciplinary Risks from Private Tutoring

| Scenario | Risk level | Likely outcome |
|----------|-----------|---------------|
| Tutoring students from another school with approval | Low | No action — compliant |
| Tutoring own students with full disclosure and approval | Medium | Managed conflict; monitor for issues |
| Tutoring own students without disclosure | High | Formal disciplinary action; possible TRBWA notification |
| Soliciting tutoring clients through school position | Very high | Serious misconduct (s 48B); likely termination |
| Poor student outcomes linked to teacher diverting content to paid tutoring | Very high | Serious misconduct + potential fraud |
| Inappropriate conduct during private tutoring of minor | Critical | Criminal referral; TRBWA cancellation; WWC Negative Notice |

## Social Media and Online Conduct

### TRBWA Professional Boundaries — Digital Context

The TRBWA professional boundaries framework applies equally to online and offline conduct. Key principles:

**Prohibited or high-risk online conduct:**
- Private social media contact with current students on personal accounts
- Sharing personal contact details (phone, personal email) with students via any platform
- Posting content that identifies students without consent
- Posting content that brings the profession into disrepute
- Using school-provided devices or accounts for personal social media
- Engaging with students' personal social media profiles (liking, commenting, following)
- Sharing content with sexual, violent, or drug-related themes where it could be seen by students or parents

**Recommended practices:**
- Maintain strict privacy settings on personal social media accounts
- Do not accept friend/follow requests from current students or recent former students
- Use only school-approved communication platforms for student contact
- Be aware that "private" posts may be screenshotted and shared
- Content posted online is effectively permanent, even if later deleted

### When Online Conduct Becomes Misconduct

The same nexus test applies. Online conduct is likely to constitute misconduct when:
- It involves or targets students
- It is visible to students, parents, or the school community
- It involves illegal activity (e.g., drug use, harassment)
- It demonstrates attitudes incompatible with the teaching role (e.g., expressed hatred of children, endorsement of violence against minors)
- It undermines public confidence in the teacher's fitness to teach

## Volunteer and Community Roles

### Teachers in Volunteer Roles

Teachers who volunteer with children outside their school (coaching sport, church groups, scouts, etc.) must:
- Maintain the same professional boundaries as in the school context
- Hold a valid WWC Card (the teacher's existing card covers volunteer roles)
- Understand that misconduct in a volunteer role can constitute a disciplinary matter under the Teacher Registration Act

### Dual Relationship Risks

When a teacher has a dual relationship with a student (e.g., teacher + sports coach, teacher + family friend), additional boundary risks arise:
- The teacher must maintain professional boundaries in both roles
- Information obtained in one role should not be used inappropriately in the other
- The power differential inherent in the teaching relationship carries across to the other role
- Any conduct concern in the non-school role may be assessed by TRBWA if it involves a student or child

### Reporting Obligations in Community Roles

**Mandatory reporting of child sexual abuse** (s 124B, Children and Community Services Act 2004) applies to teachers **at all times**, not only during school hours. A teacher who forms a belief that a child has been sexually abused — whether through their teaching role, a volunteer role, or private life — must report to the Department of Communities.
