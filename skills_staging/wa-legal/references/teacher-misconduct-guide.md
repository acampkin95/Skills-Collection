# WA Teacher Misconduct Assessor

This skill provides structured guidance for assessing teacher misconduct in Western Australia against the regulatory framework established by the **Teacher Registration Act 2012**, the **Working with Children (Screening) Act 2004**, the **Children and Community Services Act 2004**, and associated WA Department of Education policies.

It is designed for school administrators, HR professionals, investigators, legal advisors, and compliance officers who need to evaluate teacher conduct against WA regulatory thresholds and map reporting pathways, investigation processes, and possible consequence ranges.

> **Important:** This skill provides regulatory framework guidance only. It does not constitute legal advice. Complex or ambiguous matters should be referred to legal counsel, TRBWA, or the relevant regulatory body.

---

## How to use this section

This section follows a **triage-first** approach. When presented with a teacher conduct concern:

1. **Classify the conduct** — determine which regulatory category it falls into
2. **Identify reporting obligations** — who must be notified, within what timeframes
3. **Assess the threshold** — does this meet the bar for disciplinary action, and at what level
4. **Map the process** — what investigation pathway applies
5. **Map possible consequence ranges** — what sanctions or consequences may be available, without predicting a result

## Conduct classification framework

### Step 1: What type of conduct is alleged?

Map the allegation to one of these categories under the Teacher Registration Act 2012:

| Category | Legislative basis | Examples |
|----------|------------------|----------|
| **Serious misconduct** | s 48B | Sexual misconduct, physical assault, grooming, dishonest conduct, breach of trust, misuse of position |
| **Serious incompetence** | s 48A | Teaching substantially below expected standard — assessed by frequency, extent, risk, experience level |
| **Disciplinary matter** | s 47 | Breach of Act, breach of registration conditions, criminal conviction, breach of undertaking, teaching while suspended |
| **Impairment matter** | s 48 | Physical/mental health condition affecting ability to perform inherent teaching requirements |
| **Professional boundary breach** | Derived from APST + s 48B | Inappropriate communication, social media contact, one-on-one contact, gift-giving, personal disclosure |
| **Reportable conduct** | Ombudsman Act | Sexual misconduct, sexual offences, physical assault, significant neglect, psychological harm |

### Step 2: Does this trigger mandatory reporting?

Reporting obligations run in parallel — multiple notifications may be required simultaneously.

| Trigger | Report to | Timeframe | Legislation |
|---------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Belief child has been sexually abused | Department of Communities | As soon as practicable | Children and Community Services Act 2004, s 124B |
| Criminal conduct alleged (assault, sexual offences) | WA Police | Immediately | Criminal Code (WA) |
| Serious misconduct or serious incompetence suspected; teacher dismissed/suspended/resigned | TRBWA | Within **7 days** | Teacher Registration Act 2012, s 42 |
| Reportable allegation or conviction involving employee | Ombudsman WA | Within **7 working days** | Parliamentary Commissioner Act 1971 (Reportable Conduct Scheme) |
| Class 1 or Class 2 charge/conviction of WWC cardholder | WWC Screening Unit | As soon as practicable | Working with Children (Screening) Act 2004, s 29-30 |

**Penalty for failure to report to TRBWA:** $5,000 fine (s 42).
**Penalty for failure to report child sexual abuse:** Up to $6,000 fine; prosecution within 3 years.

### Step 3: Assess the disciplinary threshold

```
Is there a criminal conviction or charge?
├── Yes → Class 1 offence? → Automatic TRBWA disciplinary matter (s 47(d))
│         Class 2 offence? → Likely disciplinary matter; assess context
│         Other offence?   → Assess relevance to teaching fitness
├── No → Continue assessment
│
Does the conduct depart from the standard reasonably expected of a registered teacher?
├── No → May warrant informal management (counselling, PD plan)
├── Yes → Is it of a "serious kind"?
│         ├── Yes → Serious misconduct (s 48B) — TRBWA referral required
│         └── No  → General disciplinary matter (s 47) — employer action + possible TRBWA notification
│
Is it a pattern of incompetent practice rather than misconduct?
├── Yes → Assess frequency, extent, risk, training level
│         Substantially below standard? → Serious incompetence (s 48A)
│         Below standard but not substantially? → Performance management pathway
```

### Step 4: Identify the investigation pathway

Read `references/teacher-investigation-processes.md` for detailed procedural guidance. The summary:

**Employer-level (Department of Education / school):**
- Preliminary assessment → formal investigation (Standards and Integrity Directorate for public schools)
- Procedural fairness required throughout
- Outcomes range from counselling to termination
- Must notify TRBWA within 7 days if serious misconduct suspected and teacher dismissed/suspended/resigned

**TRBWA level:**
- Board receives complaint or employer notification
- Board investigates under s 48C-48D
- Disciplinary Committee inquiry (includes Australian lawyer)
- Standard of proof: balance of probabilities
- Outcomes: caution, reprimand, fine (max $5,000), conditions, suspension (max 2 years), cancellation

**State Administrative Tribunal:**
- Handles the most serious matters referred by TRBWA
- Fresh hearing on the merits
- Can cancel registration and disqualify teacher from reapplying

### Step 5: Map the range of possible outcomes

| Severity | Typical employer outcome | Typical TRBWA outcome | WWC impact |
|----------|------------------------|----------------------|------------|
| Minor boundary breach (first instance) | Counselling, written warning | No action or caution | None |
| Repeated boundary breaches | Formal reprimand, transfer | Conditions on registration | Unlikely unless child safety risk |
| Serious professional misconduct (non-sexual) | Suspension, possible termination | Reprimand to suspension (up to 2 years) | Reassessment triggered; outcome depends on child safety risk |
| Sexual misconduct with student | Immediate suspension, termination | Registration cancellation, disqualification | Negative Notice highly likely — conduct indicates unacceptable risk |
| Criminal conviction (Class 1 offence) | Termination | Registration cancellation | Automatic Negative Notice (s 12(5) WWC Act) |
| Criminal conviction (Class 2 offence) | Termination likely | Registration cancellation likely | Negative Notice unless exceptional circumstances |
| Grooming behaviour (established) | Suspension, investigation, likely termination | Cancellation or suspension + conditions | Reassessment triggered; Negative Notice probable |
| Serious incompetence (pattern) | Performance management → termination if unresolved | Conditions, suspension, or cancellation | Unlikely unless students placed at risk of harm |

## Professional boundaries — quick reference

Teachers must maintain clear boundaries. Breaches exist on a spectrum from inadvertent to predatory. When assessing a boundary concern, consider intent, pattern, power dynamics, and impact on the student.

**High-risk boundary violations** (likely to meet serious misconduct threshold):
- Private social media contact with students on personal accounts
- One-on-one meetings in private/unobservable spaces
- Sharing personal emotional/relationship problems with students
- Physical contact beyond incidental or safety-related
- Gift-giving to individual students (especially if concealed)
- Personal phone/text communication outside school channels
- Providing alcohol or drugs to students
- Sexual or romantic communication of any kind

**Grooming indicators** (read `references/teacher-investigation-processes.md` for full assessment framework):
- Excessive attention or "favouritism" toward a specific student
- Progressive boundary testing (jokes → personal topics → isolation)
- Exploiting vulnerable students (isolated, low self-esteem, family difficulties)
- Creating dependency or special relationship dynamics
- Normalising physical contact through gradual escalation

## WWC ban threshold — teacher misconduct nexus

The critical question: **when does a TRBWA finding lead to a WWC ban?**

Since the **2022 Amendment Act** (effective 1 July 2023), TRBWA disciplinary findings automatically trigger WWC reassessment via s 17A of the Working with Children (Screening) Act 2004.

The WWC Screening Unit then applies this test: **does the person pose an unacceptable risk of harm to children?**

**Automatic Negative Notice (no discretion):**
- Conviction for Class 1 offence as an adult

**Negative Notice highly probable:**
- Sexual misconduct with a student (any age)
- Grooming behaviour established on balance of probabilities
- Physical abuse of students
- Conviction for Class 2 offence (unless exceptional circumstances)

**Negative Notice possible — depends on assessment:**
- Serious professional misconduct not directly involving students
- Conviction for Class 3 offence involving indecent act
- Pattern of behaviour indicating risk to children even without direct harm

**Negative Notice unlikely:**
- Administrative misconduct (fraud, dishonesty not involving children)
- Serious incompetence (unless students were placed at physical risk)
- Single boundary breach promptly addressed with no pattern

Read `references/teacher-wwc-framework.md` for the full offence classification system and assessment criteria.

## Making a complaint — quick reference

Any person can make a written complaint to TRBWA about a teacher's conduct (s 51). There is no fee and no formal requirements beyond written form with sufficient detail.

**Complaint pathways (escalating):**
1. **School level** — class teacher, year coordinator, principal (aim for resolution within 14 days)
2. **Regional office** — if school resolution fails, or if the complaint involves the principal
3. **Standards and Integrity Directorate** — serious allegations (sexual offences, physical assault, significant neglect) must be reported here **without delay**
4. **TRBWA** — independent regulator; any person may lodge a public complaint (s 51)
5. **External agencies** — Ombudsman WA, WA Police, CCC, Commissioner for Children and Young People

**Whistleblower protection:** Available under the Public Interest Disclosure Act 2003 (WA) for reporting misconduct where internal channels have failed or the complainant fears reprisal.

Read `references/teacher-complaints-and-evidence.md` for full complaint procedures, evidence gathering guidance, and FOI processes.

## Evidence gathering — quick reference

**Key evidence types:** contemporaneous notes, email/message exports with metadata, witness statements (signed and dated), photographs, medical records, school records (via FOI).

**Recording conversations:** Under the Surveillance Devices Act 1998, a parent **can** record their own conversation with a teacher (one-party consent, s 5(3)) if reasonably necessary to protect lawful interests. Publication remains restricted (s 9).

**Standard of proof:** Balance of probabilities — "more likely than not." The *Briginshaw* principle applies: more serious allegations require more cogent evidence, but the standard does not change.

**Multiple instances:** Pattern evidence strengthens cases significantly. Minor incidents can aggregate to serious misconduct. Each employer notification to TRBWA (s 42) must be made separately, creating a documented pattern.

Read `references/teacher-complaints-and-evidence.md` for detailed evidence preservation, admissibility standards, and aggregation mechanisms.

## Conduct scope and conflicts — quick reference

### In-school vs out-of-school conduct

The Teacher Registration Act does not limit misconduct to school premises or hours. The **nexus test** determines whether private conduct is connected to professional fitness:
- **Strong nexus:** criminal convictions involving children, sexual offences, violence, CSAM
- **Moderate nexus:** repeated DUI, domestic violence with conviction, fraud
- **Weak/no nexus:** minor private disputes, minor regulatory infringements

The Spent Convictions Act 1988 does **not** protect teachers — s 6(1)(ba) excludes child-related employment.

### Conflicts of interest

Teachers must disclose actual, potential, or perceived conflicts of interest. Key risk areas:
- Financial interests in school vendors or service providers
- Teaching/assessing own children or relatives
- Secondary employment creating scheduling or reputational conflicts
- Private tutoring of own students (high-risk conflict)

### Private tutoring

- WA public school teachers must obtain **written secondary employment approval** before tutoring
- Tutoring own students is a high-risk conflict of interest — strongly discouraged
- Soliciting tutoring clients through school position is likely serious misconduct
- External tutors on school premises must hold a current WWC Card

Read `references/teacher-conduct-scope-conflicts.md` for the full nexus test framework, conflict management obligations, and tutoring risk matrix.

## Common Mistakes

- Treating a complaint narrative as a finding before investigation.
- Assuming reportable conduct, mandatory reporting, TRBWA, WWC, and employer notifications are interchangeable.
- Using old policy version numbers or annual-report statistics without checking current sources.
- Inferring grooming, intent, or serious misconduct from a single ambiguous event without pattern, context, and source quality review.
- Ignoring procedural fairness before adverse employment or regulatory steps.
