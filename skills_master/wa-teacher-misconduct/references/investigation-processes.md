## Department of Education Internal Processes

### Standards and Integrity Directorate

The central body within the Department of Education responsible for investigating teacher misconduct in WA public schools.

**Contact:**
- Level 2, 151 Royal Street, East Perth WA 6004
- Phone: (08) 9264 4740
- Complaints Advice Line: 1800 655 985

**Governing policy:** Check the current Department of Education policy portal before relying on version numbers. Earlier source material identified Staff Conduct and Discipline Policy v5.1 (effective 13 June 2023) and Complaints and Notifications Policy v2.1 (effective 19 November 2024), but policy versions, procedures, and forms can change.

### Complaint Categorisation

When a complaint is received, it is assessed and categorised:

**Preliminary assessment matters:**
- Lower complexity complaints
- Can be resolved informally through discussion
- Managed at school/regional level
- May involve mediation, counselling, or informal corrective action

**Investigation matters:**
- Complex and sensitive allegations
- Formal investigation by Standards and Integrity Directorate
- Structured process with documented evidence collection
- Interviews recorded with digital voice recorder

### Immediate Notification Triggers

Managers and principals must **immediately** notify Standards and Integrity Directorate when allegations involve:
- Sexual offences or sexual misconduct
- Physical assault
- Significant harm to children
- Criminal conduct
- Serious breach of duty of care

### Suspension Pending Investigation

Where a teacher's conduct poses a risk to students or others, the Department may suspend the teacher pending investigation outcome. Suspension can be:
- With pay (standard during investigation)
- Without pay (in more serious cases, subject to industrial provisions)

The teacher should be notified of the reasons for suspension and the expected investigation timeframe.

### Investigation Process Steps

1. **Complaint received** → acknowledged within 2 working days
2. **Initial assessment** → categorise as preliminary or formal investigation
3. **Investigation plan** → scope, witnesses, evidence to gather
4. **Evidence collection** → documents, interviews (recorded), physical evidence
5. **Teacher interview** → with procedural fairness protections (see below)
6. **Analysis and findings** → on balance of probabilities
7. **Outcome determination** → by appropriate decision-maker
8. **Notification** → teacher and complainant informed of outcome
9. **External notifications** → TRBWA (within 7 days if required), police, Communities

### Outcome Options

| Outcome | When appropriate | Effect |
|---------|-----------------|--------|
| **No breach found** | Evidence does not support allegation | No action; teacher advised |
| **Counselling** | Minor breach, first instance | Informal discussion; expectations clarified |
| **Written warning/reprimand** | Moderate breach | Formal record on personnel file |
| **Transfer** | Relationship irretrievably damaged or risk management | Redeployment to another school/position |
| **Suspension** | Serious breach pending further action | Removed from duties with/without pay |
| **Termination** | Serious misconduct established | Employment ended; TRBWA notified |

### Non-Government Schools

Private and independent schools have their own internal investigation processes but are subject to the same external reporting obligations (TRBWA, Ombudsman, Communities, Police). The school's governing body is responsible for ensuring adequate investigation procedures.

## TRBWA Disciplinary Proceedings

### How Matters Reach TRBWA

1. **Employer notification (s 42)** — employer notifies TRBWA of suspected serious misconduct/incompetence, or that teacher was dismissed/suspended/resigned during or after investigation
2. **Public complaint (s 51)** — any person can lodge a written complaint
3. **Board-initiated complaint (s 51A)** — Board formulates complaint based on information received
4. **Teacher self-notification (s 38-40)** — teacher notifies Board of charges, convictions, or other relevant changes
5. **Criminal history check (s 43-44)** — Board discovers relevant criminal history

### TRBWA Investigation Powers (Sections 48C-48D)

The Board may:
- Require the teacher to provide information or documents
- Require the teacher to attend for interview
- Obtain information from employers, police, and other authorities
- Commission independent investigations
- Require health assessments (for impairment matters)

### Proceeding Through the Disciplinary System

```
Complaint or notification received
    ↓
Board assessment — is there a disciplinary matter to investigate?
    ↓
    ├── No → No further action; complainant advised
    ├── Yes, but less serious → Disciplinary Committee inquiry
    └── Yes, very serious → Refer directly to SAT
         ↓
Disciplinary Committee inquiry
    ↓
    ├── No disciplinary matter found → Dismissed
    ├── Disciplinary matter found → Sanctions imposed (s 69)
    └── Matter too serious for Committee → Refer to SAT
         ↓
SAT hearing (fresh hearing on merits)
    ↓
SAT determination → sanctions up to cancellation + disqualification
    ↓
Appeal to Supreme Court (on questions of law only)
```

### Employer Notification Requirements (Section 42)

Schools must provide written notice to TRBWA within **7 days** where there are reasonable grounds to suspect a teacher has engaged in serious misconduct or taught with serious incompetence, **and** the teacher is dismissed, suspended, or ceases employment.

**Required information:**
- Teacher's full name and registration number
- Specific dates and locations of alleged conduct
- Educational institution where employed
- Date employer formed reasonable grounds
- Investigation commencement date
- Dismissal, suspension, or resignation date
- Details of other authorities notified (police, Communities)

**Penalty for non-compliance:** $5,000 fine.

**Critical note:** The notification obligation applies even when the teacher **resigns** during or after investigation. Schools cannot avoid reporting by accepting a resignation.

## Mandatory Reporting Pathways

### Child Sexual Abuse — Department of Communities

**Legislation:** Children and Community Services Act 2004, s 124B

**Who must report:** Teachers are **mandatory reporters** of child sexual abuse. This means any person registered under the Teacher Registration Act 2012 working in a teaching role (paid or voluntary).

**When to report:** When the teacher forms a **reasonable belief** that a child has been or is being sexually abused.

**How to report:**
- Mandatory Reporting Online Form
- Email to Department of Communities
- Phone contact to the local Communities office

**Penalty for failure to report:** Up to $6,000 fine; prosecution possible within 3 years of failure.

**Protection for reporters:** Good faith reports are protected — the reporter cannot be subject to civil or criminal liability, or employment action, for making a report in good faith (s 42A, Teacher Registration Act 2012 also provides similar protection for notifications to the Board).

### Criminal Conduct — WA Police

All allegations involving potential criminal conduct (sexual assault, indecent behaviour, physical assault, supply of drugs, etc.) should be reported to WA Police. The school/employer should contact:
- Local police station
- Sex Assault Squad (for sexual offences)
- Child Abuse Squad (for child abuse matters)

**Important:** Police investigation takes priority. Where police request that an employer defer its internal investigation to avoid compromising the criminal investigation, the employer should comply but must still notify TRBWA within the required timeframes.

### TRBWA Notification

See Section 42 requirements above. Key point: notification is required within **7 days** regardless of whether police or Communities investigations are ongoing.

### Corruption and Crime Commission (CCC)

For serious misconduct in the WA public sector (including public school teachers), the CCC has jurisdiction. Allegations of serious corruption, abuse of power, or systemic failures in handling misconduct should be reported to the CCC.

## Ombudsman Reportable Conduct Scheme

### Overview

The Reportable Conduct Scheme commenced **1 January 2023** and applies to government and non-government schools. It is administered by the Ombudsman WA under the Parliamentary Commissioner Act 1971.

### What Is Reportable Conduct?

Heads of organisations (school principals, governing bodies) must notify the Ombudsman of:
- **Sexual offences** committed against, with, or in the presence of a child
- **Sexual misconduct** involving a child
- **Physical assault** of a child
- **Significant neglect** of a child
- **Behaviour causing significant emotional or psychological harm** to a child

### Reporting Requirements

| Requirement | Detail |
|-------------|--------|
| **Who reports** | Head of organisation (principal, CEO of school system) |
| **Report to** | Ombudsman WA |
| **Timeframe** | Within **7 working days** of becoming aware |
| **What to report** | Allegations, convictions, prescribed offences involving employees |
| **Investigation required** | Organisation must conduct investigation and report findings |

### Reportable Sexual Misconduct Examples

- Inappropriate touching of a child
- Sharing pornographic images with a child
- Sexual comments without legitimate educational reason
- Expressing sexual desire toward a child
- Grooming behaviour (as defined below)

### Relationship to Other Reporting

The Ombudsman reportable conduct scheme operates **in parallel** with other reporting obligations. Reporting to the Ombudsman does not satisfy obligations to report to TRBWA, police, or Communities — all applicable notifications must be made.

## Professional Boundaries Framework

### Foundational Principle

The teacher-student relationship is one of trust, authority, and power imbalance. Professional boundaries exist to protect students from exploitation and to maintain the integrity of the educational relationship.

### Boundary Categories

**1. Physical Contact**
- Acceptable: brief, appropriate contact for safety, comfort (young children), or instructional purposes (e.g., sports coaching with consent)
- Unacceptable: hugging, kissing, massage, prolonged physical contact, contact in private settings, contact that the student has indicated is unwanted

**2. Communication**
- Acceptable: school-approved communication channels (school email, learning management systems), transparent group communication
- Unacceptable: private social media messaging, personal phone/text communication, encrypted or disappearing message apps, communication concealed from school administration

**3. Social Media**
- Teachers must **reject** student requests to connect on personal social media accounts
- Professional social media use should be through official school accounts only
- Sharing personal life details on social media viewable by students is discouraged

**4. One-on-One Contact**
- Should occur in observable, non-private settings (door open, window in door, visible to others)
- Avoid being alone with a student in an isolated or unobservable location
- Where one-on-one support is educationally necessary, inform colleagues and maintain transparency

**5. Personal Disclosure**
- Teachers should not share personal emotional, relationship, or financial problems with students
- Students are not appropriate sources of emotional support for teachers
- Personal disclosure creates inappropriate intimacy and erodes professional boundaries

**6. Gift-Giving**
- Teachers should not give gifts to individual students (especially if concealed from other staff or parents)
- Accepting gifts from students/families should follow school policy
- Excessive or personal gift-giving is a grooming indicator

**7. Outside-School Contact**
- Private meetings with students outside school require institutional knowledge and approval
- Transporting individual students in personal vehicles is high-risk and generally discouraged
- Social contact with students outside school context should be avoided unless part of approved school activities

**8. Favouritism and Special Treatment**
- Treating one student differently from others without educational justification is a boundary concern
- Creating exclusive relationships or "special" status for particular students is a grooming indicator

### Boundary Breach Spectrum

Not all boundary breaches are equal. Assessment should consider:

```
Inadvertent/Minor ←――――――――――――――――→ Deliberate/Serious
│                                                    │
│ Accepted friend request               Initiated private
│ without thinking                       sexual communication
│                                                    │
│ Shared personal anecdote              Created secret
│ in class discussion                    relationship with student
│                                                    │
│ Drove student home in                 Pattern of isolating
│ emergency (reported)                   student from peers
```

## Grooming Indicators and Assessment

### What Is Grooming?

Grooming is the process by which a person in a position of trust gradually conditions a child (and often the child's environment) to accept increasingly inappropriate behaviour, ultimately aimed at sexual exploitation.

In the teacher-student context, grooming exploits the authority, trust, and access inherent in the teaching role.

### Grooming Stages

**Stage 1 — Targeting**
- Identifying vulnerable students (isolated, low self-esteem, family difficulties, seeking adult attention/approval)
- Assessing which students are less likely to report or be believed

**Stage 2 — Gaining Trust**
- Becoming the student's confidant, advocate, or "favourite teacher"
- Offering special help, attention, or privileges
- Positioning self as understanding and different from other adults

**Stage 3 — Filling a Need**
- Providing emotional support, gifts, or material assistance
- Creating dependency (the student "needs" the teacher)
- Becoming the go-to person for the student's problems

**Stage 4 — Isolating the Child**
- Creating opportunities for private contact
- Undermining the student's other relationships (peers, family, other staff)
- Establishing secret communication channels
- Manufacturing situations where the student is alone with the teacher

**Stage 5 — Desensitising**
- Introducing sexual content gradually (jokes, images, topics)
- Normalising physical contact through progressive escalation
- Testing the student's reaction to boundary violations
- Reframing inappropriate behaviour as "normal" or "special"

**Stage 6 — Maintaining Control**
- Using guilt, shame, or fear to prevent disclosure
- Threatening consequences if the student tells
- Reinforcing the "special" nature of the relationship
- Creating complicity ("we're in this together")

### Risk Factors — Teacher Perpetrators

- Personal or emotional difficulties (relationship breakdown, isolation, stress)
- Social or emotional immaturity
- Seeking attention, admiration, or emotional validation from students
- History of boundary issues (even minor ones)
- Resentment of professional constraints
- Identification with student peer groups rather than adult colleagues

### Risk Factors — Vulnerable Students

- Experiencing discrimination or social isolation
- Low self-esteem or sense of self-worth
- Weak or absent parental relationships
- Lack of close parental supervision
- History of abuse or neglect
- Compliance traits or difficulty asserting boundaries
- Students with disabilities that increase dependency on adults
- Students going through identity challenges (LGBTQI+ students in unsupportive environments)
- Students experiencing family crisis (divorce, death, financial hardship)

### Assessing Grooming Allegations

When evaluating whether conduct constitutes grooming, consider:

1. **Pattern:** Is there a progression of boundary violations over time?
2. **Targeting:** Did the teacher focus on a vulnerable student?
3. **Isolation:** Were steps taken to create private access?
4. **Secrecy:** Was the conduct concealed from colleagues, parents, or administration?
5. **Escalation:** Did the nature of contact become increasingly inappropriate?
6. **Power dynamics:** Was the teacher-student authority relationship exploited?
7. **Intent:** While intent matters, grooming can also occur through reckless disregard for boundaries rather than deliberate predation

**Important:** Grooming behaviour can meet the threshold for serious misconduct (s 48B) even where no sexual contact has occurred. The conduct itself — the progressive boundary erosion targeting a child — is the misconduct.

## High School and College Specific Considerations

### Age and Developmental Context

Secondary students (typically 12-18) present particular risks and considerations:

- **Adolescent vulnerability:** Identity formation, peer influence, desire for adult recognition, and physical/emotional changes create susceptibility to grooming
- **Digital literacy mismatch:** Students are digitally fluent but may lack judgment about online relationships with authority figures
- **Age of consent complexity:** In WA, the age of consent is 16, but **a sexual relationship between a teacher and a student of any age is professional misconduct** regardless of consent laws. The power imbalance inherent in the teacher-student relationship means "consent" in this context is compromised.

### Specific Risk Areas for Secondary Schools

**Social media and digital communication:**
- Students may initiate contact via social media — the teacher's responsibility is to maintain the boundary regardless
- Private messaging apps (Snapchat, Instagram DMs) create unmonitored communication channels
- Group chats can transition to private messaging
- Teachers should use only school-approved communication platforms

**Extra-curricular activities:**
- Camps, excursions, and sporting trips create increased proximity and reduced formal structure
- One-on-one coaching, tutoring, or mentoring sessions require transparency and oversight
- After-school activities may involve less supervision than regular teaching hours

**Year 11-12 and college settings:**
- Students approaching or past 18 may blur the boundary between "student" and "adult" — the professional obligation remains unchanged
- University pathway mentoring can create inappropriate dependency
- Social events (school balls, end-of-year celebrations) require clear professional conduct

**Post-school contact:**
- Teachers should exercise caution about contact with recently graduated students
- The transition from teacher-student to any other relationship type should involve a significant cooling-off period
- Where a teacher taught a student, the power dynamic does not disappear at graduation

### Institutional Safeguards

Schools should implement:
- Clear professional boundaries policy communicated to all staff
- Regular training on boundary awareness and grooming recognition
- Anonymous reporting mechanisms for students and staff
- Supervision structures that reduce opportunities for isolated contact
- Digital communication policies that mandate use of school platforms
- Incident documentation systems that track patterns across multiple reports

## Investigation Timeframes

| Stage | Timeframe | Source |
|-------|-----------|--------|
| Complaint acknowledgement | Within 2 working days | DoE Complaints Policy |
| Service delivery complaint resolution | Aim for 14 days | DoE Complaints Policy |
| Conduct complaint — formal investigation | No specified maximum; typically weeks to months | DoE Staff Conduct Policy |
| TRBWA employer notification | Within **7 days** of dismissal/suspension/resignation | Teacher Registration Act s 42 |
| Ombudsman reportable conduct notification | Within **7 working days** of becoming aware | Reportable Conduct Scheme |
| TRBWA interim disciplinary order referral to SAT | Within **14 days** | Teacher Registration Act s 59 |
| Mandatory report to Communities (child sexual abuse) | As soon as practicable | Children and Community Services Act s 124B |
| Report to police (criminal conduct) | Immediately | General obligation |
| Full TRBWA disciplinary process (complaint to determination) | Typically **6-18 months** | TRBWA practice |
| SAT review application (WWC Negative Notice) | Within **28 days** of notice | WWC Screening Act s 26 |
| Application to cancel Negative Notice | After **3 years** (or earlier if grounds exist) | WWC Screening Act s 19 |
| Prosecution limitation — failure to report child sexual abuse | **3 years** | Children and Community Services Act |

### Concurrent Investigations

Multiple investigations can run simultaneously:
- Police criminal investigation (takes priority — employer may defer internal investigation if requested)
- Department of Education internal investigation
- TRBWA disciplinary proceedings
- Ombudsman reportable conduct investigation
- WWC reassessment

**Key principle:** Deferral of internal investigation at police request does not defer the obligation to notify TRBWA. The s 42 notification must be made within 7 days regardless of other proceedings.

## Procedural Fairness Requirements

### Core Principles

All investigations and disciplinary processes must observe procedural fairness (also called natural justice):

**1. Hearing rule** — The teacher must be:
- Informed of the specific allegations in sufficient detail to respond
- Given a reasonable opportunity to respond before any adverse decision
- Allowed to present evidence and call witnesses
- Allowed to have a support person or representative present

**2. Bias rule** — Decision-makers must:
- Have no direct personal interest in the outcome
- Not have prejudged the matter
- Approach the evidence with an open mind

**3. Evidence rule** — Decisions must be:
- Based on relevant evidence (balance of probabilities standard)
- Not based on irrelevant considerations
- Supported by logical reasoning connecting evidence to findings

### Practical Application

- Provide the teacher with written details of the allegation
- Allow adequate time to prepare a response (what is "adequate" depends on complexity — generally at least 7-14 days for a formal investigation)
- Conduct interviews in a professional manner; record interviews
- Allow the teacher to have a union representative, lawyer, or support person present
- Provide the teacher with an opportunity to comment on adverse evidence before findings are made
- Document the reasoning for findings and outcomes
- Notify the teacher of the outcome in writing, including reasons and any appeal rights

### When Procedural Fairness May Be Modified

- **Interim suspension:** Where there is an immediate risk to students, a teacher may be suspended before the full investigation is complete — but the teacher must still be given an opportunity to respond as soon as practicable
- **Criminal investigation:** Where police request that the employer not alert the teacher, procedural fairness obligations may be temporarily deferred — but must be observed once the police restriction is lifted
