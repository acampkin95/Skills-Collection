# Mental Health Act 2014 (WA) — FVRO Intersections

> **DISCLAIMER**: General legal information only. Not legal advice. Obtain independent legal advice from a WA lawyer, Legal Aid WA, or community legal centre.

## MHA Treatment Conflict — Detailed Variation Guidance

### When This Applies

A person bound by an FVRO AND subject to an involuntary treatment order under the *Mental Health Act 2014* (WA) faces a conflict when:

1. **Hospital location**: Inpatient treatment hospital is within FVRO exclusion zone
2. **Clinic location**: Community treatment clinic is within FVRO exclusion zone
3. **Treatment schedule**: MHA appointments conflict with FVRO conditions (e.g., curfew, movement restrictions)
4. **Discharge location**: Post-discharge supported accommodation falls within exclusion zone

### Step-by-Step Process

#### Step 1: Inform Treating Psychiatrist Immediately

- Tell your treating psychiatrist about the FVRO and the exclusion zone conflict
- Request a letter from the psychiatrist supporting a variation application (see letter template below)
- The psychiatrist should confirm: diagnosis, treatment plan, clinic location, necessity of attendance, risk of non-attendance, no alternative services available

#### Step 2: File Application to Vary FVRO (s.45)

- File with the Magistrates Court that made the FVRO
- Use Form [applicable court form] — Application to Vary Restraining Order
- Attach affidavit (see Template 5 in affidavit-templates.md)
- Attach psychiatrist's letter as exhibit

#### Step 3: Attend Leave Hearing (s.46)

- Hearing is ex parte (protected person not present)
- Argue hardship ground — s.46(4)(a)(iii): "restraints are causing serious and unnecessary hardship"
- Present evidence of MHA treatment conflict
- Propose specific, narrow variation (clinic attendance only, strict times, direct route)

#### Step 4: If Leave Granted — Substantive Hearing

- Both parties may attend
- Court considers proposed variation against protected person's safety
- Court may impose conditions on the variation (supervised attendance, time restrictions, reporting)

### Treating Psychiatrist Letter Template

The treating psychiatrist's letter should include:

```
[Letterhead — Public/Private Psychiatric Practice]

To the Magistrates Court of Western Australia at [location]

Re: [Patient Full Name] — DOB [date]

I am [full name], a registered psychiatrist practising at [clinic/hospital
name, address]. I have been treating [patient name] since [date].

DIAGNOSIS AND TREATMENT

[Patient name] has been diagnosed with [diagnosis] under the Mental Health
Act 2014 (WA). [He/She] is currently subject to an involuntary [community
patient order / inpatient treatment order] made on [date].

[His/Her] treatment plan requires attendance at [clinic name] at [address]
on [frequency, e.g., twice weekly — Tuesdays and Fridays] for [treatment
type, e.g., medication administration, psychiatric review, psychotherapy].

TREATMENT NECESSITY

Continued attendance at this clinic is clinically necessary because:
- [Patient]'s condition requires [ongoing medication monitoring / regular
  psychiatric review / supervised treatment];
- Non-attendance would place [patient] at significant risk of [relapse /
  symptom exacerbation / hospitalisation / deterioration in functioning];
- [Patient]'s current stability depends on consistent engagement with
  treatment.

LOCATION CONFLICT

I understand that [patient]'s Family Violence Restraining Order includes
an exclusion zone that encompasses the clinic at [address]. This creates
a direct conflict between [his/her] mental health treatment obligations
and [his/her] FVRO compliance.

ALTERNATIVE SERVICES

[There are no alternative mental health services available within
reasonable distance that can provide the specialised treatment [patient]
requires.]

OR

[While [alternative service] exists at [location], it does not provide
[specific treatment] and transfer would [disrupt continuity of care /
delay treatment / require re-assessment].]

CLINICAL OPINION

In my clinical opinion, variation of the FVRO exclusion zone to permit
[patient] to attend this clinic for treatment appointments would be
in the interests of [his/her] mental health and public safety. Continued
treatment substantially reduces the risk factors associated with
[his/her] condition.

I am available to provide further information or appear as a witness
if required by the court.

[Signature, name, qualifications, registration number]
[Date]
```

## Cancellation Based on Mental Health Remission — Detailed Guidance

### When This Applies

A respondent with a final FVRO applies to cancel under s.46(4)(a)(ii) (substantial change), arguing that their mental health condition — which contributed to the original family violence — has since been successfully treated.

### Linking Mental Health to Original FVRO

The affidavit must establish a causal chain:

1. **At the time of the FVRO**: Respondent was experiencing [untreated/poorly managed mental illness]
2. **Mental illness contributed to the conduct**: The family violence was connected to or a symptom of the psychiatric condition (e.g., psychotic episode, manic episode, substance-induced psychosis)
3. **Since the FVRO**: Respondent has engaged with treatment; condition is now managed
4. **Current status**: Mental illness is in remission / well-controlled; risk factors that contributed to family violence have been addressed
5. **Therefore**: Substantial change in the circumstances that led to the FVRO

### Evidence Hierarchy (Strongest to Weakest)

| Rank | Evidence Type | Weight |
|------|-------------|--------|
| 1 | **Forensic psychiatric assessment** — independent expert | Very High |
| 2 | **Treating psychiatrist's letter** — detailed clinical opinion | High |
| 3 | **Hospital discharge summaries** — documenting treatment trajectory | High |
| 4 | **Treatment attendance records** — consistent engagement | Medium-High |
| 5 | **Medication compliance records** — pharmacy records, blood tests | Medium |
| 6 | **Behaviour change program certificates** — completed programs | Medium |
| 7 | **GP letters** — general health/medication confirmation | Medium |
| 8 | **Counsellor/psychologist letters** — therapy progress | Medium |
| 9 | **Self-report** — respondent's own account | Low |

### Key Paragraphs for Mental Health Cancellation Affidavit

```
MENTAL HEALTH HISTORY

[X]. At the time the FVRO was made on [date], I was experiencing

     episodes, severe depression with psychotic features].

     to / a symptom of / exacerbated by] my untreated mental illness.
     During [acute psychotic episodes / manic episodes / periods of
     untreated illness], I [describe connection between symptoms and

     (a) [Date]: Commenced treatment with [psychiatrist / service].
     (b) [Date]: [Hospitalised for [X weeks]. Medication regimen

     (c) [Date]: [Completed inpatient program. Discharged on [medication]].
     (d) [Date]: [Transitioned to community mental health care at [service]].

     (a) Diagnosed with [stable/well-controlled condition, e.g.,

     (b) On [medication regimen, e.g., "Risperidone 3mg daily and

     (c) Attending [clinic/service] [frequency, e.g., fortnightly];
     (d) In remission from acute symptoms — no hospitalisations in
         [X years/months];
     (e) Functioning at [describe — employment, study, daily activities,
         stable accommodation].

     which confirms:
     (a) My current diagnosis and treatment status;
     (b) That my mental health has significantly improved since the FVRO;
     (c) That I am compliant with medication and treatment;
     (d) That the psychiatric risk factors associated with the original

     (e) That, in [his/her] clinical opinion, the risk of further family

     a manifestation of] my untreated mental illness. That illness is
     now treated and stable. The behavioural risk factors that the court
     was concerned about when making the FVRO have been substantially
     mitigated through [X years/months] of consistent treatment,
     medication compliance, and rehabilitation. This constitutes a
     substantial change in the relevant circumstances since the order
     was made.
```

## Dual MHA/FVRO Breach — Sentencing Considerations

### When Respondent Breaches FVRO While Subject to MHA Order

**Aggravating factors the prosecution may raise**:
- Pattern of non-compliance (both MHA and FVRO)
- Risk to protected person increased by respondent's non-engagement with treatment
- Previous breach history

**Mitigating factors the defence may raise**:
- Respondent was acutely unwell at time of breach (psychiatric evidence)
- Symptoms directly impaired capacity to understand/follow FVRO conditions
- Breach was symptom-driven, not volitional
- Respondent has since re-engaged with MHA treatment
- Respondent has demonstrated willingness to comply (compliance history before breach)

**Key case law consideration**: *Dominik v Volpi* [2004] WASCA 18 held that "the need for general and individual deterrence will ordinarily outweigh subjective or other mitigating considerations." Mental health mitigation therefore faces a high bar — but psychiatric evidence from a treating or forensic psychiatrist demonstrating incapacity at the time of breach may be persuasive.

### Evidence Checklist for Mental Health Breach Mitigation

- [ ] Forensic psychiatric assessment (or treating psychiatrist report) addressing:
  - Diagnosis at time of breach
  - Symptoms present at time of breach
  - Whether symptoms impaired capacity to understand FVRO obligations
  - Whether breach was symptom-driven or volitional
  - Current status and prognosis
- [ ] Hospital admission records (if admitted around time of breach)
- [ ] MHA treatment records showing compliance history (before and after breach)
- [ ] Medication records (was respondent on medication? Was there a lapse?)
- [ ] Evidence of re-engagement with treatment since breach
- [ ] Any pre-existing communication with MHA services about FVRO compliance difficulties
